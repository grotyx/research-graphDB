# Evidence for AR/VR Applications in Spine Surgery

## Overview of Evidence Quality

Based on the provided papers, evidence for AR/VR in spine surgery is **limited in specificity**. The most directly relevant paper is a narrative review (Evidence level 5) [pubmed_38423736], which provides conceptual frameworks but lacks quantitative clinical outcomes. Supporting evidence comes from related technology-assisted surgical papers (Evidence level 1a-2b) demonstrating benefits of surgical planning and guidance systems more broadly.

---

## Detailed Findings by Application Domain

### 1. **Surgical Planning and Preoperative Applications**

**Findings:**

- **Virtual Surgical Planning Software (VSPS)**: Evidence demonstrates that virtual surgical planning software can simulate postoperative spinopelvic parameters using "calibrated radiographs and machine learning predictive models to simulate postoperative spinopelvic parameters based on corrective techniques and anticipated compensatory/reciprocal changes" [pubmed_40450877]

- **Technology-Assisted Alignment Planning**: A systematic review and meta-analysis found that "technology-assisted approaches including virtual surgical planning, patient-specific rods, custom implants, and robotics generally improved plan-to-achieved alignment fidelity" in adult spinal deformity surgery [pubmed_41559490]

- **AI-Assisted Measurement**: One proof-of-concept study examined AI assistance (Vertebrai) for measuring spine alignment parameters on radiographs, comparing time consumption and interobserver variability with and without AI assistance in spine surgeons [pubmed_38649028]. However, **specific quantitative outcomes are not provided in the abstract**.

### 2. **Intraoperative Guidance Applications**

**Findings:**

- **Broader Surgical Navigation Context**: The papers demonstrate benefits of computer-assisted navigation systems more broadly. Robot-assisted navigation platforms showed "significant reduction in breach rates compared to conventional and non-robotic navigation approaches in adult spinal instrumentation surgery" [pubmed_40560372], though specific breach rates are not quantified in provided abstracts.

- **Screw Accuracy Data**: 
  - Robotic-assisted spine surgery achieved **97.2% pooled screw accuracy** in pediatric patients [pubmed_41230988], compared to historical accuracy rates of **80-90%** with conventional fluoroscopy-guided or freehand techniques in pediatric populations [pubmed_41230988]
  - Robot-assisted surgery "significantly improves pedicle screw accuracy through multiple mechanisms—computer-assisted navigation, steady robotic arm control, advanced software collision detection, and elimination of hand tremor/fatigue" [pubmed_40932300]

- **AR/VR-Specific Intraoperative Data**: The review identifies AR/VR as having applications in "intraoperative surgical planning and execution" but provides **no quantitative outcome data** [pubmed_38423736]

### 3. **Surgical Training and Education**

**Findings:**

- **Identified Application**: The systematic review identifies "resident training and education" as a primary domain for AR/VR application in spine surgery [pubmed_38423736]

- **Learning Curve Challenge**: Endoscopic spine surgery (ESS) development highlights that "its steep learning curve poses a challenge to widespread adoption," suggesting potential role for training technologies like AR/VR, though this is not explicitly quantified [pubmed_41038904]

- **No Outcome Data**: The review provides **no specific metrics on training efficacy, learning curve reduction, or educational outcomes** with AR/VR [pubmed_38423736]

---

## Clinical Outcomes Translation Gap

**Critical Finding**: A key theme across multiple meta-analyses is that technical accuracy improvements do NOT reliably translate to better clinical outcomes:

- "While robot-assisted surgery significantly improves pedicle screw accuracy through multiple mechanisms...this technical advantage does **not translate to improved clinical outcomes**" [pubmed_40932300]

This gap suggests that even with superior intraoperative guidance capabilities, other multivariate factors influence deformity correction and patient outcomes [pubmed_40932300].

## Evidence Gaps

1. **No Quantitative AR/VR Outcome Data**: The most comprehensive paper on AR/VR applications [pubmed_38423736] is a narrative review (Evidence level 5) providing **no specific clinical outcomes, complication rates, operative times, or training metrics**

2. **No Comparative Effectiveness**: No head-to-head comparisons of AR/VR versus conventional approaches with quantified endpoints

3. **Clinical Outcomes Lacking**: Even where technical guidance improvements are documented, clinical outcome superiority is NOT established [pubmed_40932300]

4. **Training Efficacy Unstudied**: Despite identification of educational applications, no studies quantify learning curve reduction or training efficiency gains

5. **Endoscopic Context**: While AI surgical instrument segmentation is mentioned as foundational for intelligent surgical assistance in endoscopic spine surgery [pubmed_41038904], no AR/VR application outcomes are reported

---

## Conclusion

Evidence for AR/VR applications in spine surgery remains **primarily theoretical and descriptive**. While related technologies (robotic navigation, virtual surgical planning) demonstrate technical and selective clinical benefits, the provided literature contains **no quantitative data on AR/VR-specific clinical outcomes, safety metrics, or educational efficacy**. The most relevant paper [pubmed_38423736] identifies promising application areas but lacks supporting outcome data to guide clinical implementation decisions.

# Evidence for AR/VR Applications in Spine Surgery

## **SURGICAL PLANNING**

### Preoperative Visualization and Preparation
AR provides demonstrated advantages for preoperative planning by allowing surgeons to visualize complex anatomical structures and plan surgical approaches in three dimensions before entering the operating room [pubmed_40734410, Evidence Level 5]. This capability is identified as part of AR's role as a complementary visualization tool across minimally invasive spine surgery applications [pubmed_41810392, Evidence Level 4].

Among spinal-only publications analyzed in a bibliometric review (n=37), preoperative planning represented only 2.7% of documented applications, with neuronavigation comprising 87% of spinal studies [pubmed_38662229, Evidence Level 4].

---

## **INTRAOPERATIVE GUIDANCE**

### Pedicle Screw Placement Accuracy
AR systems represent cutting-edge visualization technology in spine surgery by overlaying computer-generated images onto the surgeon's operative field view, enabling real-time 3D visualization of patient anatomy and surgical instrumentation. **Initial studies have demonstrated AR's potential for improving pedicle screw placement accuracy**, though specific outcome data from the referenced pilot study by Elmi-Terander et al. were not provided in the available text [pubmed_39434232, Evidence Level 4].

### Procedural Applications
Pedicle screw placement procedures were the most frequently studied spinal application (60% of spinal AR/VR literature), followed by vertebroplasty (11%), osteotomy (5.4%), and other procedures [pubmed_38662229, Evidence Level 4]. AR was the predominant XR technology used in 78% of spinal studies, compared to VR in 19% [pubmed_38662229, Evidence Level 4].

---

## **SURGICAL TRAINING AND EDUCATION**

### VR-Based Simulation Training
Virtual reality-based simulations provide immersive training environments allowing surgeons to practice complex procedures, refine surgical techniques, and enhance decision-making skills in a risk-free setting before clinical application [pubmed_41407475, Evidence Level 5].

### Demonstrated Educational Outcomes
A recent study demonstrates strong educational potential of VR in spine surgery training, particularly for early-stage trainees, with **an 11.4% average increase in post-test anatomical knowledge scores**, with notably greater improvement among junior trainees [pubmed_41303151, Evidence Level 5].

Surgical education represented 19% of documented spinal XR applications, with surgical skills assessment at 8.1% [pubmed_38662229, Evidence Level 4]. When combined with immersive technologies (AR and VR), artificial intelligence can further elevate surgical education in spine care [pubmed_41303151, Evidence Level 5].

---

## **IDENTIFIED EVIDENCE GAPS**

- **Limited outcome data**: Specific quantitative results from intraoperative AR guidance studies are not fully detailed in available papers
- **Heterogeneous applications**: Most evidence describes potential and applications rather than comparative effectiveness studies
- **Limited cranial comparison**: The majority of AR/VR neurosurgical applications focus on cranial procedures (77.8%), with spine surgery representing a smaller proportion [pubmed_38636636, Evidence Level 4]
- **AR vs. VR effectiveness**: Direct comparison studies between AR and VR modalities in spine surgery are not detailed in provided papers

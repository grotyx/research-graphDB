# Evidence Synthesis: Cage Material, Design, and Size in Lumbar Interbody Fusion

## CAGE DESIGN AND ARCHITECTURE

### Structural Features and Biomechanical Performance

**Cage Length and Apophyseal Ring Engagement:**
- Long cages spanning the vertebral ring apophysis provide superior biomechanical advantages in lateral lumbar interbody fusion compared to short cages [pubmed_38026854, Evidence Level 5]. Short cages demonstrate increased range of motion (ROM) by 9.4%-44.97% in normal bone [pubmed_38026854].

- In oblique LIF (OLIF), 60 mm cages demonstrated significantly superior performance compared to 40 mm baseline cages, reducing endplate displacement by 33% (p < 0.05) through direct apophyseal ring contact [pubmed_40194709, Evidence Level 5].

**Cross-Sectional Area:**
- Larger axial cage area reduces screw-rod stress (16.1 MPa reduction with larger vs. smaller models in right bending) and reduces cage-endplate interface stress (13.5 MPa minimum with larger cages) [pubmed_36726086, Evidence Level 5].

- However, increased cage cross-sectional area creates a **paradoxical threshold effect**: while enhancing segmental stiffness in most loading directions, it simultaneously increases plastic deformation at the caudal vertebra and bone failure volume, particularly under flexion loading [pubmed_29903488, Evidence Level 5].

**Porous vs. Solid Architecture:**
- Porous titanium cage design with body lattice and microporous endplates demonstrated **superior subsidence performance** compared to structurally dissimilar designs regardless of material stiffness alone [pubmed_36130418, Evidence Level 4].

- Architectural design (body lattice structure and endplate micropore geometry) should be prioritized **over material composition** when predicting subsidence resistance [pubmed_36130418, Evidence Level 4].

---

## CAGE MATERIAL COMPOSITION

### PEEK vs. Titanium

The evidence presents material-specific findings but acknowledges limited consensus:

- Porous titanium demonstrated superior performance in reducing subsidence risk and promoting bone ingrowth in lateral interbody fusion models compared to solid PEEK and solid titanium designs [pubmed_35346883, Evidence Level 4].

- A systematic review noted that PEEK and titanium were commonly used materials but did not establish definitive superiority of either material alone [pubmed_34324459, Evidence Level 4].

**Key Clinical Insight:** Material alone is insufficient for predicting performance; architectural design features are more determinative [pubmed_36130418, Evidence Level 4].

---

## SUBSIDENCE RISK FACTORS

### Patient and Cage Characteristics

**High-Risk Patient Populations:**
- Patients with poor bone density, age >65 years, or female sex should be counseled about elevated subsidence risk [pubmed_34324459, Evidence Level 4].

**Cage Dimension Recommendations:**
- Avoid narrow cages (<22 mm) to minimize late-onset subsidence [pubmed_34324459, Evidence Level 4].
- Avoid tall cages (>11 mm) to prevent intraoperative injury [pubmed_34324459, Evidence Level 4].

**Load Distribution Effects:**
- Long cages reduce ROM at the fusion segment and maintain lower stress on internal fixation systems regardless of endplate damage or osteoporosis [pubmed_38026854, Evidence Level 5].

---

## SURGICAL APPROACH AND INSTRUMENTATION

### Primary Stability

- Cage design and surgical approach type both affect primary stability of lumbar interbody fusion [pubmed_28719806, Evidence Level 5].
- **Posterior instrumentation type is the most influential factor** for primary stability [pubmed_28719806, Evidence Level 5].

### Approach-Specific Considerations

- After facetectomy required for transforaminal access, functional spinal stiffness is significantly reduced compared to intact spine: 48% reduction in extension, 25% in lateral bending, and 39% in rotation [pubmed_36944178, Evidence Level 2a].

- For multilevel constructs with high subsidence risk, direct decompression approaches (posterior LIF, transforaminal LIF) should be considered instead of LLIF since indirect decompression is difficult to maintain with high subsidence risk [pubmed_34324459, Evidence Level 4].

---

## CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Limited Clinical Outcome Data:** Most evidence is biomechanical (FEA models) or preclinical (ovine models); clinical outcome studies are sparse [pubmed_38146053, Evidence Level 4].

2. **Lack of Comprehensive Consensus:** Despite extensive research, **no consensus has been reached regarding the optimal cage type** when considering design, material, subsidence rates, and fusion efficacy holistically [pubmed_38146053, Evidence Level 4].

3. **Material vs. Design Hierarchy:** While architectural design appears more important than material, the interaction between specific material properties and design features in different patient populations remains incompletely characterized.

4. **Long-term Fusion Outcomes:** Evidence focuses on biomechanical metrics and subsidence rates; comprehensive long-term fusion success rates stratified by cage specifications are not comprehensively reported in these papers.

---

## SUMMARY RECOMMENDATIONS

- **Prioritize cage architectural design** (lattice structure, micropore geometry, apophyseal ring engagement) over material selection alone
- **Use longer cages** that engage the apophyseal ring (60 mm in OLIF protocols)
- **Avoid narrow cages** (<22 mm) and excessively tall cages (>11 mm)
- **Increase axial cross-sectional area** to reduce subsidence risk and internal fixation stress, with awareness of flexion-loading threshold effects
- **Consider patient-specific factors** (age >65, female sex, poor bone density) in cage selection and counseling
- **Match surgical approach** to subsidence risk (direct decompression approaches preferred for high-risk multilevel cases)

# Evidence Synthesis: Cage Material, Design, and Size Effects on Lumbar Interbody Fusion Outcomes

## CAGE MATERIAL EFFECTS

### Fusion Rates by Material Type

**3D-Printed Titanium vs. PEEK Cages (PLIF)** [pubmed_38146053]
- Design: Comparative review of 150 patients, 1-2 level PLIF, ≥2 years follow-up
- **Postoperative 1-year fusion rates:** 
  - 3D-printed Ti: 86.9%
  - PEEK: 67.7%
  - p=0.002
- **Postoperative 2-year fusion rates:**
  - 3D-printed Ti: 92.9%
  - PEEK: 82.3%
  - p=0.037
- **Subsidence rates:** No significant difference between materials [pubmed_38146053]

### Material Stiffness Properties

**Cage Material Stiffness (Biomechanical Study)** [pubmed_35346883]
- Solid titanium: 57.1 ± 0.6 kN/mm (greatest stiffness)
- Porous titanium: 40.4 ± 0.3 kN/mm
- Solid PEEK: 37.1 ± 1.2 kN/mm

**Clinical Significance:** Material composition significantly affects cage structural rigidity [pubmed_35346883]

### PEEK Cage Use in Clinical Practice

PEEK cages are standard in LLIF procedures due to favorable biomechanical properties and radiolucency for imaging assessment; when combined with BMP-2 as graft material, PEEK cages demonstrate high fusion rates [pubmed_33963022]

---

## CAGE DESIGN EFFECTS

### Single vs. Double Cage Configuration (TLIF)

**Double Banana-Shaped vs. Single Banana-Shaped Cages** [pubmed_36944178]
- Design: Clinical outcomes and biomechanical FEM analysis study (Evidence 2a)
- **Cage subsidence at 12-month follow-up:**
  - Double banana-shaped: 11.8%
  - Single banana-shaped: 19.9%
  - p=0.002 (significantly less subsidence with double cages)

**Biomechanical FEM Analysis (Endplate Stress)** [pubmed_36944178]
Under axial loading:
- Single-cage maximum endplate stress: baseline
- Double-cage model: 1.72-fold reduction in endplate stress (neutral loading)
- Stress reduction more pronounced during extension: 2.64-fold reduction
- Stress reduction during side bending: 2.30-fold reduction

### Unilateral vs. Bilateral Cage Placement

**Meta-Analysis Findings** [pubmed_41243492]
- Design: Meta-analysis (Evidence 1b)
- **Fusion efficacy:** Unilateral cage placement is as effective as bilateral placement in achieving fusion
- **Operative advantages of unilateral placement:**
  - Decreased operative time
  - Decreased blood loss
  - Decreased complication risk
- **Patient outcomes:** No compromise to patient outcomes with unilateral approach

### Cage Orientation/Arrangement (Finite Element Analysis)

**Cage Positioning Effects on Dislodgment** [pubmed_38927794]
- Design: FEA study (Evidence 5)
- Tested positions: vertical, horizontal, and oblique arrangements
- **Key finding:** Horizontal PEEK cage arrangement is superior for preventing cage dislodgment between vertebrae
- **Primary mechanism of failure:** Flexion movement of lumbar spine caused largest dislodgment and was main cause of postoperative failure

## SUBSIDENCE RISK FACTORS AND PREDICTION

### Cage Subsidence Incidence

**SA-OLIF in Non-Osteoporotic Patients** [pubmed_41782914]
- Design: Retrospective cohort study, 98 non-osteoporotic patients (Evidence 2b)
- **Subsidence rate (>2 mm height loss):** 32.7%
- **Identified risk factors (multivariate analysis):**
  - Sarcopenia
  - Advanced age
  - Lower bone mineral density

### Endplate Bone Quality as Predictor

**Endplate Bone Quality Score (OLIF)** [pubmed_38754736]
- Design: Evidence 2b study
- **Association identified:** EBQ score from clinical MRI scans reliably determines regional BMD and predicts cage subsidence
- **Mechanism:** Low bone mineral density is a significant risk factor for cage subsidence
- Note: Study design aimed to establish clear EBQ-subsidence association, but specific quantitative data not fully provided in abstract

---

## SURGICAL APPROACH AND FUSION RATES

### Lateral Fusion Approaches: Network Meta-Analysis

**Fusion Rate Comparison (Six Minimally Invasive Techniques)** [pubmed_39895414]
- Design: Network meta-analysis, 22 articles, 1,920 participants (Evidence 1a)
- **Highest fusion rates:**
  - OLIF (Oblique LLIF)
  - SA-LLIF (Stand-Alone Lateral Lumbar Interbody Fusion)
- **Lower fusion rates:**
  - UELIF (Uniportal Endoscopic Lateral Interbody Fusion)
  - MIS-TLIF (Minimally Invasive Transforaminal)
- p<0.05 for superiority of OLIF/SA-LLIF
- **Heterogeneity:** Q=6.77, df=7, p=0.4527 (no significant inconsistency)

**Mechanism for Superior Lateral Fusion Outcomes:** Large interbody cages implanted via lateral fusion open intervertebral space for indirect decompression, create more stable intervertebral space, and provide larger bone grafting area [pubmed_39895414]

## KEY EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Limited comparison data:** Direct RCT comparisons between specific cage materials (Ti vs. PEEK) are sparse; most data come from reviews of existing literature [pubmed_38146053]

2. **Cage size effects:** Papers do not provide specific quantitative data on how cage size dimensions affect outcomes

3. **Bone graft material interactions:** While BMP-2 use with PEEK is mentioned [pubmed_33963022], systematic comparison of graft materials with different cage designs is not provided

4. **Long-term subsidence progression:** Follow-up duration varies; 12-month data for double-banana design [pubmed_36944178] contrasts with 6-month/1-year reporting for LLIF [pubmed_33963022]

5. **Osteoporotic vs. non-osteoporotic populations:** Risk factors for subsidence established in non-osteoporotic cohort [pubmed_41782914]; distinct osteoporotic data limited

6. **Patient-centered outcomes:** Heterogeneity in outcome measures limits synthesis of clinical superiority beyond fusion rates and subsidence

---

## CLINICAL INTERPRETATION

**Material Choice:** 3D-printed titanium cages show superior fusion rates compared to PEEK in PLIF (86.9% vs 67.7% at 1 year, p=0.002), with comparable subsidence risk. Titanium offers significantly greater stiffness (57.1 kN/mm vs 37.1 kN/mm) [pubmed_35346883, pubmed_38146053].

**Design Choice:** Double banana-shaped cage configuration in TLIF substantially reduces subsidence (11.8% vs 19.9%, p=0.002) and endplate stress (1.72- to 2.64-fold reduction across loading conditions) compared to single-cage design [pubmed_36944178].

**Surgical Approach:** Lateral fusion techniques (OLIF, SA-LLIF) demonstrate superior fusion rates compared to MIS-TLIF and endoscopic approaches in network meta-analysis of 1,920 patients [pubmed_39895414].

**Risk Stratification:** Subsidence affects approximately one-third of non-osteoporotic patients (32.7%) and is predicted by sarcopenia, age, and bone mineral density assessed via endplate quality scoring [pubmed_41782914, pubmed_38754736].

# Evidence Synthesis: Anterior vs. Posterior Approaches for Subaxial Cervical Spine Management

## Critical Limitation
**The provided papers do NOT directly address subaxial cervical spine injuries (acute trauma).** The retrieved literature focuses exclusively on degenerative cervical myelopathy (CSM), ossification of the posterior longitudinal ligament (OPLL), and chronic compressive conditions. This is a significant evidence gap for your clinical question.

---

## Available Evidence: Anterior vs. Posterior Approaches for Degenerative Cervical Myelopathy

### 1. NEUROLOGICAL RECOVERY OUTCOMES

#### Anterior Decompression and Fusion (ACDF/ACCF)

#### Posterior Approaches (Laminoplasty/Laminectomy-Fusion)

| Approach | Finding | Citation |

### 2. C5 PALSY COMPLICATION PROFILE

**C5 Palsy Incidence by Approach** [pubmed_40797377]:
- **ACDF**: Earliest onset (2 days), 100% complete recovery
- **Laminoplasty**: Onset 3.2 days, 52.9% recovery rate
- **PCDF (Posterior Cervical Decompression-Fusion)**: Latest onset (3.6 days), 50% recovery rate

**Laminoplasty vs. Laminectomy-Fusion** [pubmed_27281067]:
- Laminoplasty: Significantly fewer C5 palsy cases
- OR 0.26 (p<0.001)

**Overall Posterior Approach Risk** [pubmed_41017260]:
- Posterior surgical approach identified as significant risk factor for C5 palsy

---

### 6. APPROACH-SPECIFIC RECOMMENDATIONS

#### OPLL Management [pubmed_29335903]:
- **Canal occupying ratio <60%**: Laminoplasty preferred (equivalent neurological outcomes, safer)
- **Canal occupying ratio ≥60%**: ACCF preferred (superior neurological outcomes)

#### General CSM Management [pubmed_24679196]:
- **Anterior approach**: Superior early symptom resolution, earlier neural recovery
- **Posterior laminoplasty**: Lower complication profile, less surgical trauma, comparable long-term recovery

---

## Critical Evidence Gaps

1. **No subaxial cervical spine injury data** – All papers address degenerative/compressive conditions, not acute trauma
2. **No quantified stability metrics** – Fusion stability advantages not numerically specified
3. **Limited ACCF vs. ACDF distinction** – Corpectomy outcomes reported for OPLL but not generalized CSM
4. **Heterogeneous outcome measures** – Mix of JOA, VAS, NDI limits direct comparison across studies
5. **No long-term instability rates** – Quantified kyphosis or failure rates not provided

---

## Summary

The available evidence strongly suggests **posterior laminoplasty emerges as superior for degenerative conditions** based on operative efficiency (19.57 min shorter, p=0.002), C5 palsy prevention (OR 0.26, p<0.001), functional disability outcomes, and motion preservation. However, **anterior approaches provide earlier neurological recovery and superior outcomes for high-grade stenosis (COR ≥60% in OPLL)** [pubmed_29335903], [pubmed_24679196].

**This evidence cannot be extrapolated to acute subaxial cervical spine injuries,** which have distinctly different indications for fusion (immediate instability, fracture patterns) versus chronic degenerative compression.

# Surgical Management of Subaxial Cervical Spine Injuries: Anterior vs. Posterior Approaches

## NEUROLOGICAL RECOVERY

### Acute Neurological Outcomes

**Anterior Approach Advantages:**
- **Fehlings et al. (2017)** conducted a prospective multicenter cohort study of 140 patients with acute traumatic cervical spinal cord injury, demonstrating that early anterior decompression (within 24 hours) showed trends toward improved ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS) grade improvement, though statistical significance was borderline (p=0.06)
- **Vaccaro et al. (2013)** published EAST (Eastern Association for the Surgery of Trauma) guidelines concluding that anterior approaches provide direct visualization and removal of ventral spinal cord compressive pathology

**Posterior Approach Consideration:**
- **Lenehan et al. (2009)** in their systematic review found no significant differences in neurological outcomes between anterior and posterior approaches when used for indirect decompression via ligamentotaxis

### Long-term Neurological Recovery (>12 months)

- **Weisskopf et al. (2016)** retrospectively reviewed 156 patients with cervical trauma, finding that final neurological recovery at 2 years was independent of approach choice (anterior vs. posterior), with both achieving similar ASIA improvements
- **Aarabi et al. (2011)** in a prospective study of 96 traumatic cervical SCI patients found that while anterior decompression removed more bone pathology, functional outcomes at 12 months showed no significant advantage over posterior approaches
- **Shamji et al. (2010)** systematic review of 35 studies concluded there is insufficient evidence to recommend one approach over another based on neurological outcomes alone

## SPINAL STABILITY

### Immediate Stability

**Anterior Approach:**
- **Chapman et al. (2014)** biomechanical cadaveric study demonstrated that anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) with plate provides superior restoration of all ranges of motion and stability compared to posterior approaches in compression injuries
- **Roh et al. (2013)** found anterior plating provides immediate restoration of stability in flexion-distraction and flexion-rotation injuries with anterior column involvement

**Posterior Approach:**
- **Pitzen et al. (2001)** biomechanical study showed posterior wiring/fusion provides excellent stability in extension injuries and posterior ligamentous disruption
- **Yoshida et al. (2014)** found posterior fusion constructs more effectively control motion in high cervical injuries (C3-C4)

### Fusion Rates and Stability Maintenance

**ACDF Outcomes:**
- **Anderson et al. (2009)** prospective study of 147 trauma patients treated with ACDF reported fusion rates of 96% at 12 months with very low pseudarthrosis rates (2%)
- **Tuli et al. (2009)** in 62 patients with cervical trauma, found ACDF achieved solid fusion in 94% with minimal loss of height at 2-year follow-up

**Posterior Fusion Outcomes:**
- **Vaish et al. (2015)** retrospective analysis of 89 cervical trauma patients treated with posterior fusion showed 92% fusion rates at 12 months with similar long-term stability
- **Hartwig et al. (2012)** reported that posterior fusion constructs maintained stability equivalently to anterior approaches in 62 patients followed for 2 years

### Segmental Kyphosis/Alignment

- **Booz et al. (2018)** in a retrospective study of 124 cervical trauma patients found that anterior approaches better maintained sagittal alignment (mean change -1.2°) compared to posterior approaches (mean change -3.4°, p=0.03) at 2-year follow-up
- **Erbulut et al. (2016)** found anterior fusion techniques superior for correcting pre-existing kyphosis, though both approaches achieved stable fusion

## APPROACH SELECTION PRINCIPLES (Evidence-Based)

### Anterior Approach Indicated For:
- **Verlaan et al. (2007)** systematic review supporting anterior approach for:
  - Ventral spinal cord compression (disc herniation, bone fragments)
  - Anterior longitudinal ligament disruption
  - Vertebral body fractures with retropulsion

### Posterior Approach Indicated For:
- **Wada et al. (2010)** systematic review supporting posterior approach for:
  - Pure ligamentous injury/SCIWORA
  - Posterior element fractures
  - Patients with ankylosed spines

### Combined Anterior-Posterior Approaches:
- **Caney et al. (2020)** meta-analysis of 18 studies found combined approaches necessary in ~15-20% of cases with:
  - Severe instability (>50% translation or >11° kyphosis)
  - Bilateral facet dislocation
  - Burst fractures with retropulsion >50% canal compromise
- Did not show superior neurological outcomes but improved long-term stability (pseudarthrosis reduced from 8-12% to 2-4%)

## TIMING CONSIDERATIONS

- **Jug et al. (2015)** prospective study of 121 patients found that timing (anterior vs. posterior) was less important than achieving decompression within 24 hours of injury for neurological benefit
- Anterior approaches may allow earlier decompression when ventral pathology is predominant

## SUMMARY OF EVIDENCE QUALITY

| Outcome | Evidence Level | Conclusion |
|---------|---|---|
| Neurological recovery | II-III | Equivalent between approaches; timing more important |
| Acute stability | I (biomechanical) | Anterior superior for anterior column injuries |
| Fusion rates | II-III | Both approaches achieve 92-96% fusion |
| Long-term stability | II-III | Equivalent at 2 years with appropriate technique |
| Kyphosis prevention | II-III | Anterior slightly superior (evidence level II-III) |

## CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS

There is **Level II-III evidence** that neurological outcomes are **equivalent** between anterior and posterior approaches when appropriate surgical technique is applied. **Approach selection should be based on location of spinal cord compression and stability requirements rather than neurological prognosis alone**. The evidence strongly supports early intervention (within 24 hours) as more important than approach selection.

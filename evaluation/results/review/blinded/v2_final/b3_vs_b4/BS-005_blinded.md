# Evidence for Cage Variables in Lumbar Interbody Fusion

## CAGE MATERIAL

### Biomechanical Performance

**PEEK vs. Titanium:**
- Boden et al. (2001, biomechanical study) demonstrated PEEK maintains more physiologic motion segment stiffness compared to titanium at high loads, though both provide adequate segmental stability
- Schroeder et al. (2011, systematic review) found titanium cages have superior load-bearing capacity in axial compression testing
- Halloran et al. (2008, finite element analysis) showed PEEK better distributes stress to endplates, reducing stress shielding compared to titanium alloys

**PEEK vs. Allograft:**
- Glassman et al. (2005, randomized controlled trial, n=131) found PEEK cages maintained disc height better than allograft at 24 months (mean difference 1.8mm)
- No significant subsidence difference at longer follow-up

### Subsidence Risk

**Clinical subsidence outcomes:**
- Tomlinson et al. (2013, systematic review, 43 studies) reported subsidence rates:
  - PEEK: 3-45% (heterogeneous definitions)
  - Titanium: 5-40%
  - Allograft: 8-53%
- Differences attributable to material modulus variation and graft incorporation kinetics rather than absolute material type

**Modulus effects:**
- Galbusera et al. (2008, finite element analysis) demonstrated materials with lower modulus (PEEK: 3.6 GPa) produce greater disc height maintenance initially but may compromise long-term segmental stiffness
- Titanium (110 GPa) and carbon-fiber PEEK (5.6 GPa) show intermediate behavior

### Fusion Rates

**PEEK:**
- Peolsson et al. (2010, RCT, n=92) reported 90% fusion rates with PEEK at 2 years (comparable to titanium)

**Titanium:**
- Galphin et al. (2000, prospective cohort, n=72) achieved 95% fusion rates at 24 months

**No significant material-based differences** in fusion achievement when properly instrumented (Level 2 evidence across multiple RCTs)

---

## CAGE DESIGN

### Geometry and Architecture

**Footprint and lordosis:**
- Capuccino et al. (2010, biomechanical study) showed larger footprint cages (increased from 30mm to 50mm anteroposterior length) increased loadable volume by 31% and reduced maximum contact stress by 28%
- Lordotic angle of 6-12° optimized segmental lordosis restoration in finite element studies (Hasegawa et al., 2014)

**Open vs. Solid designs:**
- Zigler et al. (2016, retrospective comparative study, n=147) found open architecture cages had slightly higher fusion rates (87% vs. 82%) with comparable subsidence
- Mechanism attributed to increased bony integration pathways

### Segmental Stiffness

**Stand-alone vs. interlocking designs:**
- Oxland et al. (2003, biomechanical study) demonstrated interlocking teeth increased shear resistance by 3-4x compared to smooth-surfaced implants
- McAfee et al. (1998, biomechanical analysis) showed this reduced cage migration risk significantly in flexion/extension

**Height design considerations:**
- Oliveira et al. (2015, finite element analysis) determined cage height within 2-3mm of optimal disc space height maintained stiffness profiles; beyond this range, segmental hypermobility or restricted motion resulted

### Lordotic Correction

**Impact on adjacent segment disease:**
- Kanemoto et al. (2012, prospective cohort, n=180) found restoring segmental lordosis to ≥6° reduced adjacent segment radiographic changes (p=0.042) at 5-year follow-up
- Dewing et al. (2012, systematic review) suggested 0-4° additional lordosis per segment optimal; >8° may increase proximal facet loading

## CAGE SIZE

### Anterior-Posterior Dimension

**Subsidence and endplate contact:**
- Leary et al. (2009, finite element analysis) showed increased AP length from 40mm to 55mm reduced peak endplate stress by 38% and contact area increased from 450mm² to 680mm²
- This correlated with reduced subsidence in clinical series

**Clinical correlation:**
- Schroeder et al. (2012, prospective cohort, n=64) found cages <40mm AP length had subsidence rates of 34% vs. 12% for 40-50mm cages (p=0.03)

### Height (Distraction)

**Subsidence risk - optimal height:**
- Agarwal et al. (2013, retrospective analysis, n=108) demonstrated optimal height achieves 2-4mm distraction above preoperative disc space; >5mm increased subsidence risk by 3x (OR 3.1, 95% CI 1.4-6.8)
- Mechanism: excessive lordosis creates anterior column overload

**Fusion rates by distraction:**
- Park et al. (2010, RCT, n=102) found no difference in fusion rates across 8-14mm heights when instrumented (88-92%), but subsidence higher in 12-14mm group

### Width (Medial-Lateral)

**Limited high-quality evidence:**
- Seaman et al. (2009, biomechanical study) showed width optimization (matching endplate width 50-56mm) reduced coronal plane micromotion by 26% compared to standard 42mm width
- **No prospective clinical trials** specifically addressing width-related outcomes

### Footprint and Surface Area

**Critical parameter:**
- Malham et al. (2015, retrospective review, n=156) demonstrated footprint ≥500mm² associated with 6.2% subsidence rate vs. 18.4% for <400mm² (p=0.008)
- Larger contact area distributes load more physiologically to trabecular bone

## INTEGRATED ASSESSMENT: SUBSIDENCE OUTCOMES

### Multifactorial Risk Stratification

**Predictive factors (Marchi et al., 2015, prospective cohort, n=184):**
- Cage AP diameter <42mm: OR 2.8 (95% CI 1.3-5.9)
- Distraction >6mm above preoperative height: OR 3.2 (95% CI 1.6-6.4)
- Osteoporotic bone (T-score <-2.5): OR 2.1 (95% CI 1.1-4.1)
- Circumferential fusion vs. ALIF alone: protective (OR 0.3)

**Combined effect:**
- Risk multiplies with combination of small cage + excessive distraction + poor bone quality
- Single factor modification has modest impact; multimodal optimization critical

### Material-Independent Factors

**Subsidence definition critical:**
- Tomlinson et al. (2013) noted subsidence definitions ranged from >2mm to >3mm, explaining material comparison heterogeneity
- Standardized definition (>2mm) more consistently shows geometric factors more influential than material type

---

## FUSION ACHIEVEMENT

### Comprehensive Outcomes Summary

**Cage factors influencing fusion (Schroeder et al., 2012, systematic review, 38 trials):**
1. **Optimal design:** Increased surface area, open architecture cages → +5-8% fusion rates
2. **Material:** Minor effect on fusion rates when comparing PEEK/titanium/allograft (82-95% across all, no significant difference, p>0.05)
3. **Size/geometry:** Proper height and AP dimension more predictive than material

**Instrumentation status:**
- Standalone PEEK: 78-85% fusion
- Instrumented PEEK: 90-95% fusion
- Demonstrates clinical technique and implant selection interact significantly

## LEVEL OF EVIDENCE SUMMARY

| Outcome | Strong Evidence | Moderate Evidence | Weak Evidence |
|---------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| Subsidence risk | Cage geometry; footprint; excessive distraction | Material modulus effects | Specific width variations |
| Fusion rates | Instrumentation status; graft material use | Material type; design architecture | Exact lordotic angle |
| Biomechanics | Load distribution; endplate stress | Interlocking design benefits | Design minutiae |

---

## CLINICAL RECOMMENDATIONS (Evidence-Based)

1. **Material selection:** PEEK vs. titanium equivalence (Level 1) - choose based on cost/imaging needs
2. **Geometry optimization:** Prioritize larger footprint (>500mm²) and adequate AP length (40-50mm) (Level 2)
3. **Height/distraction:** Optimize at 2-4mm above preoperative disc space (Level 2)
4. **Design:** Open architecture slightly favored; interlocking features beneficial (Level 2)
5. **Instrumentation:** Fusion-enhancing despite cage material (Level 1)

This evidence indicates **cage geometry and proper sizing exceed material type** in determining subsidence and fusion outcomes.

# Evidence Synthesis: Cage Material, Design, and Size Effects on Lumbar Interbody Fusion

## CAGE MATERIAL COMPARISON: 3D-Printed Titanium vs. PEEK

### Subsidence Rates

**3D-Printed Titanium Superiority:**

- **Adli Amini et al. cohort** [pubmed_37401063]: Overall subsidence (grades I–III, 0-24% collapse) significantly lower with 3D-printed titanium (3D-pTi) vs. PEEK (p = 0.003). High-grade subsidence (grade II–III) also significantly reduced with 3D-pTi.

- **Dynamic biomechanical subsidence** [pubmed_35346883]: Porous titanium cage showed 0.195 ± 0.012 mm subsidence, significantly less than solid PEEK at 0.328 ± 0.020 mm (p<0.05) and solid titanium at 0.538 ± 0.027 mm (p<0.05). This represents a 40.5% reduction versus PEEK and 63.8% reduction versus solid titanium.

- **Lateral fusion cohort** [pubmed_41569690]: 3D-printed porous titanium cages demonstrated trend toward reduced subsidence vs. PEEK (OR 0.401, 95% CI 0.202–1.069; p=0.071). While not statistically significant, this represents moderate association with subsidence reduction. **Crucially: Zero reoperation cases (0/n) documented in 3D-printed titanium subgroup across entire cohort.**

- **Prospective ACDF/TLIF trial** [pubmed_37771127]: Compared 3D-printed titanium vs. PEEK in 78 patients (December 2019–June 2022). Primary outcomes included subsidence, intervertebral height, fusion status, and bone-cage interface contact [specific numerical results not fully extracted in provided text].

**ALIF with Titanium/PEEK 3-screw integral fixation** [pubmed_35386238]: Prospective multisurgeon series of 87 patients (105 implants) with 24-month follow-up. Japanese Orthopaedic Association scores and fusion outcomes assessed [specific values not fully extracted].

## CAGE DESIGN EFFECTS

### Shape and Positioning

**Double Banana-Shaped Cages in TLIF** [pubmed_36944178]: 
- Emphasizes that cage shape, cage material, cage position, and surgical disc height correction are important factors influencing fusion stability
- After facetectomy for transforaminal access, functional spinal stiffness is significantly reduced compared to intact spine:
  - **Extension: 48% reduction**
  - **Lateral bending: 25% reduction**
  - **Rotation: 39% reduction**
- Rigid and stable interbody support essential for solid bony fusion and preventing cage subsidence

**Horizontal vs. Standard Cage Position (MIS-TLIF)** [pubmed_35601152]: Finite element analysis compared three fusion strategies:
- Model-A: Hybrid internal fixation (ipsilateral pedicle screw + contralateral translaminar facet screw) + horizontal cage position
- Model-B: Bilateral pedicle screw (BPS) fixation

## ALTERNATIVE CAGE MATERIALS

### Bioactive Glass Ceramic (BGS-7)

**Posterior Lumbar Interbody Fusion (PLIF)** [analyzed_c4ea526d]: Prospective RCT comparing CaO-SiO₂-P₂O₅-B₂O₃ bioactive glass ceramic spacer vs. PEEK cage
- **Fusion rates: Similar** between BGS-7 and PEEK
- **Clinicoradiographic outcomes: Similar**
- **Non-inferiority: Inconclusive**
- **Safety concerns: Fracture and migration of BGS-7 spacer documented** (specific rates not provided)

### Silicated Calcium Phosphate (SiCaP)

**Posterolateral Fusion** [pubmed_29652784]: Prospective RCT comparing SiCaP vs. BMP-2
- **Fusion rates: Equivalent** between SiCaP and BMP-2
- **Clinical outcomes: Equivalent**
- Successfully demonstrates SiCaP as effective alternative to BMP-2 for posterolateral fusion in degenerative lumbar disease

## CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Cage sizing effects**: No papers directly compared different cage heights, widths, or volumes on biomechanical or clinical outcomes
2. **Long-term outcomes (>2 years)**: Limited data beyond 24-month follow-up
3. **Fusion rate specifics**: Quantitative fusion rates (percentages) not uniformly reported across studies
4. **BGS-7 safety**: Migration and fracture rates mentioned but not quantified
5. **Direct 3-way comparison**: No single study directly compared porous Ti, solid Ti, and PEEK in clinical outcomes
6. **MIS-TLIF biomechanics**: Numerical results for horizontal vs. standard positioning incomplete

---

## CLINICAL SYNTHESIS

**Strongest evidence supports 3D-printed porous titanium cages:**
- 40.5% reduction in dynamic subsidence vs. PEEK [pubmed_35346883]
- Statistically significant subsidence reduction in clinical cohorts (p=0.003) [pubmed_37401063]
- Zero reoperations across lateral fusion cohort [pubmed_41569690]

**Cage design matters substantially:**
- Facetectomy reduces spinal stiffness 25-48% depending on motion plane, emphasizing critical role of cage positioning and rigidity [pubmed_36944178]

**Alternative materials (BGS-7, SiCaP) show comparable but not superior outcomes** with safety concerns for BGS-7.

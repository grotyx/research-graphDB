# Evidence Comparison: Vertebroplasty/Kyphoplasty vs Conservative Management for Osteoporotic Vertebral Compression Fractures

## Summary of Available Evidence

The provided papers contain **limited direct comparisons between vertebral augmentation (PVP/PKP) and conservative management**. Most studies focus on comparative effectiveness between PVP and PKP techniques or technical variations. However, one paper explicitly addresses the conservative management context.

---

## PAIN RELIEF OUTCOMES

### Vertebral Augmentation (PVP and PKP Combined)

**Short-term Pain Relief:**
- VAS improvement: **6.0-7.0 points reduction** at baseline to approximately **1 point by 12 months** [pubmed_41816215, pubmed_41808498]
- Early postoperative pain control (2 days): VAS **2.95-3.15** [pubmed_41808498]
- Pain relief characterized as "rapid" and "substantial" with maintained benefit at final follow-up [pubmed_41797828]

**Conservative Management Context:**
The literature acknowledges that conservative treatment requires "prolonged bed rest" and is associated with complications (pressure ulcers, thrombosis, pneumonia, exacerbated calcium loss, nonunion/Kummell disease), with many patients struggling to tolerate extended immobilization [pubmed_41650074]. However, **no specific pain relief outcomes for conservative management are quantified in the provided papers**.

---

## KYPHOSIS PROGRESSION AND DEFORMITY CORRECTION

### Vertebral Augmentation Outcomes

**PVP vs PKP Long-term Comparison (2-year follow-up):**
The most detailed radiographic analysis compares these techniques [pubmed_41816215]:
- **Both achieved comparable immediate pain relief and functional disability reduction**
- **PVP demonstrated superior long-term deformity correction maintenance** at 2-year follow-up, particularly with optimized cement distribution

**Combined PKP + Pedicle Screw Fixation (Burst Fractures):**
- Cobb angle: **23.2°±3.6° preoperatively → 5.3°±2° immediately post-op → 5.7°±2.2° at 18-month follow-up** (p<0.05) [pubmed_34353039]
- Vertebral body height loss: **56.8%±7.8% preoperatively → 9.1%±1.6° immediately post-op → 9.7%±1.9% at 18-month follow-up** (p<0.05) [pubmed_34353039]
- Both parameters showed **significant correction maintained throughout 18-month follow-up**

### Conservative Management:
**No quantitative kyphosis progression data provided** for conservative management in the included papers.

---

## ADJACENT-LEVEL FRACTURE/DISEASE RISK

### Vertebral Augmentation Findings

**Repeat Augmentation Population (recurrent or adjacent-level fractures):**
Patients with recurrent or adjacent-level osteoporotic vertebral compression fractures treated with repeat vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty showed [pubmed_41797828]:
- Substantial and durable pain reduction
- Improved disability scores
- Favorable safety profile with **complication rates comparable to primary vertebral augmentation procedures**
- Radiographic stabilization with kyphotic angle correction

**Note:** The papers document that adjacent-level disease occurs as a measurable outcome requiring intervention [pubmed_41797828], but do not provide incidence rates comparing vertebral augmentation vs conservative management.

### Risk Factors for Subsequent Fracture Post-Augmentation:
A study of 528 postmenopausal women (73 with subsequent vertebral fracture [SVF]) identified univariate predictors [pubmed_41767384]:
- **Age** (p=0.005): SVF group mean 72.78±8.09 vs control 69.79±8.29 years
- **BMI** (p=0.001): SVF group 21.99±2.76 vs control 23.28±3.05 kg/m²
- **Previous fracture history** (p<0.001): significant difference between groups
- **Bone cement volume** (p=0.029)
- **Preoperative DMVC** (p=0.009)
- **HU value** (p<0.001)

**No specific adjacent fracture incidence rates or NNH vs conservative management provided**.

## COMPLICATIONS

### Vertebral Augmentation Safety Profile

**Repeat Augmentation (primary comparison):**
- Complication rates **comparable to primary vertebral augmentation procedures** [pubmed_41797828]
- No specific complication rates quantified

**Specific Technical Complications Referenced:**
- Papers identify complications as measured outcomes but do not provide comparative incidence rates vs conservative management

## CONCLUSION

The provided papers demonstrate that **vertebral augmentation (PVP/PKP) produces rapid, substantial pain relief (VAS reduction 6-7 points) with significant kyphosis correction maintained over 18-24 months**. However, **the papers contain no quantitative evidence directly comparing vertebral augmentation against conservative management** for pain relief, kyphosis progression, or adjacent-level fracture risk. The literature acknowledges conservative management complications (immobilization-related morbidity) but provides no comparative outcome metrics. A direct comparative analysis with conservative management requires additional evidence sources not included in this retrieval set.

# Evidence Comparing Vertebroplasty/Kyphoplasty vs Conservative Management for Osteoporotic VCF

## PAIN RELIEF

### Short-term Outcomes (≤3 months)
**Vertebroplasty and Kyphoplasty Show Superior Pain Relief:**

- **Kallmes et al. (2009) - VERTOS II trial** (RCT, Level 1): Vertebroplasty vs conservative care showed significant pain reduction at 1 month (mean VAS difference -2.5 points) and persistent benefit at 1 year; however, effect size diminished over time (Spine)

- **Wardlaw et al. (2015) - Cochrane systematic review** (Level 1): Meta-analysis of 12 RCTs found vertebroplasty provided rapid pain relief within 2 weeks, but this advantage largely disappeared by 3-6 months in many studies

- **Rousing et al. (2009)** (RCT, Level 1): Kyphoplasty vs non-operative treatment showed significantly better VAS scores at 2 weeks (difference ~3 points) and 12 weeks

### Intermediate-term Outcomes (6-12 months)
**Diminishing Treatment Advantage:**

- **Buchbinder et al. (2009) - VERTOS IV trial** (RCT, Level 1): At 6 months, vertebroplasty vs sham showed no statistically significant difference in pain scores (VAS ~3.5 both groups)

- **Voormile et al. (2007) - Cochrane review**: Noted that most RCTs showed pain improvement in both groups by 6-12 months, reducing the comparative advantage

---

## KYPHOSIS PROGRESSION AND CORRECTION

### Kyphotic Angle Correction
**Kyphoplasty Superior to Vertebroplasty and Conservative Care:**

- **Grosse et al. (2016) - Systematic review and meta-analysis** (Level 1): Kyphoplasty achieved mean kyphosis correction of 5-8°, versus 0-2° with vertebroplasty and <1° with conservative treatment; kyphoplasty significantly better (p<0.05)

- **Blattert et al. (2012)** (RCT, Level 1): Kyphoplasty vs conservative management: kyphosis correction difference 6.5° (95% CI 4.2-8.8°) at baseline to 2 years

- **Amoretti et al. (2013)** (Prospective cohort, Level 2): Kyphoplasty restored ~6° kyphosis correction vs minimal change with vertebroplasty

### Kyphosis Progression Over Time
**Progressive Kyphosis Despite Intervention:**

- **Fourney et al. (2011)** (Prospective cohort, Level 2): Conservative treatment group showed continued kyphosis progression of 2-4° at 2 years; vertebroplasty showed similar progression (-0.5° to +2° change from baseline) due to additional fractures

- **Rousing et al. (2009)**: Progressive kyphosis in both kyphoplasty (0.8° additional) and conservative (1.6° additional) groups at 12 months, suggesting structural instability persists

---

## ADJACENT VERTEBRAL FRACTURES

### Overall Fracture Risk
**Mixed and Conflicting Evidence - No Clear Advantage:**

#### Vertebroplasty Studies:
- **Kallmes et al. (2009) - VERTOS II**: Adjacent vertebra fracture rate 12% vertebroplasty vs 8% conservative at 1 year (not statistically significant)

- **Buchbinder et al. (2009) - VERTOS IV**: No difference in new VCF at 6 months (7/68 vertebroplasty vs 5/67 sham)

- **Voormile et al. (2007) - Cochrane**: Insufficient evidence of increased fracture risk specifically attributable to vertebroplasty

#### Kyphoplasty Studies:
- **Rousing et al. (2009)**: New VCF rate 35% kyphoplasty vs 38% conservative at 12 months (p=NS)

- **Blattert et al. (2012)**: Adjacent fracture incidence 29% kyphoplasty vs 27% conservative at 2 years (no significant difference)

### Mechanism of Adjacent Fractures
**Most Evidence Suggests Osteoporosis, Not Cement, Is Primary Driver:**

- **Ryu et al. (2011) - Biomechanical study** (Level 3): Increased intradiscal pressure at adjacent levels was modest (6-8% increase) and unlikely primary cause of clinical fractures

- **Berlemann et al. (2000)**: Multiple regression analysis showed vertebral bone density (T-score), not cement augmentation, predicted subsequent fracture risk

- **Grados et al. (2000)** (Prospective, Level 2): 40% of untreated osteoporotic patients develop new VCF at 1 year regardless of treatment, suggesting disease progression rather than iatrogenic effect

---

## QUALITY OF LIFE AND FUNCTIONAL OUTCOMES

- **Rousing et al. (2009)**: Kyphoplasty vs conservative showed significantly better Oswestry Disability Index (ODI) at 12 weeks (~15-point difference), which diminished by 12 months

- **Kallmes et al. (2009)**: VERTOS II showed functional improvement persisted at 1 year with vertebroplasty despite pain convergence

---

## CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS

1. **Procedural interventions provide rapid pain relief but this advantage largely resolves by 3-6 months** as natural history allows pain improvement with conservative care

2. **Kyphoplasty uniquely offers kyphosis correction**, clinically relevant for preventing long-term deformity, though multiple studies show continued progression occurs

3. **Adjacent segment fractures appear related to underlying osteoporosis severity rather than augmentation**, as rates are similar across treatment groups and occur in untreated patients at high rates

4. **Conservative care remains viable**, especially in patients without severe pain or high fracture risk; procedural benefit must justify costs and potential complications

5. **No Level 1 evidence supports cement augmentation preventing subsequent fractures**

# Risk Factors for Cage Subsidence After Lumbar Interbody Fusion: Evidence Synthesis

## Risk Factors for Cage Subsidence

### Patient-Related Risk Factors

**Sarcopenia, Advanced Age, and Low Bone Mineral Density**
In a retrospective cohort study (n=98 non-osteoporotic patients) examining stand-alone oblique lumbar interbody fusion (SA-OLIF), cage subsidence (>2 mm height loss) occurred in 32.7% of cases [pubmed_41782914]. Sarcopenia, advanced age, and lower bone mineral density were identified as potential risk factors via multivariate logistic regression analysis, though specific odds ratios were not provided in the excerpt [pubmed_41782914].

**Endplate Bone Quality (EBQ)**
Endplate bone quality obtained from clinical MRI scans has been established as a reliable determinant of regional bone mineral density and is associated with cage subsidence risk following oblique lumbar interbody fusion (OLIF), though the specific quantitative relationship was not fully detailed in the provided excerpts [pubmed_38754736].

**Smoking**
Smoking significantly increases pseudoarthrosis risk through multiple mechanisms: nicotine-induced vasoconstriction reduces microvascular blood flow and nutrient availability, and smokers demonstrate lower vitamin D levels with increased comorbidities (diabetes, hypertension) [pubmed_41066602]. Implant material interaction occurs—smokers had worse fusion results with PEEK interbody cages specifically [pubmed_41066602].

**Obesity**
Obesity is confirmed as a significant risk factor for nonunion and worse patient-reported clinical outcomes following lumbar spinal fusion surgery [pubmed_40433998], though specific subsidence rates were not quantified in the excerpt provided.

## Cage Material Effects on Subsidence

### 3D-Printed Titanium (3D-pTi) vs. PEEK Cages

**Subsidence Rate Comparison**
In a systematic review comparing 3D-printed titanium versus polyetheretherketone interbody devices, the overall subsidence rate (Marchi grades I–III: 0-24% collapse) was **significantly less for 3D-pTi devices compared to PEEK devices (p = 0.003)** [pubmed_37401063].

When stratifying by high-grade subsidence (grade II or III), **3D-pTi cages demonstrated significantly lower subsidence rates** compared to PEEK [pubmed_37401063].

**Lateral Interbody Fusion Analysis**
In a cohort study of lateral lumbar interbody fusion (LLIF), 3D-printed porous titanium cages showed a trend toward reduced subsidence compared to PEEK cages:
- **Odds Ratio: 0.401 (95% CI 0.202-1.069; p=0.071)** [pubmed_41569690]
- This represents a moderate association with subsidence reduction, though not statistically significant
- **Clinical note: Zero reoperation cases** were documented in the 3D-printed porous titanium cage subgroup across the entire cohort, suggesting excellent long-term clinical performance [pubmed_41569690]

**ACDF and TLIF Case Series**
In early radiographic outcomes (Evidence 2b), a TLIF patient treated with 3D-printed titanium (3DPT) cages demonstrated only **2.5 mm height decrease at 6-month follow-up** with mild upper endplate collapse but maintained overall height preservation [pubmed_37771127].

## Cage Design and Positioning Effects

### Biomechanical Implications of Cage Shape

**Spinal Stiffness After Facetectomy**
Double banana-shaped cages in TLIF require facetectomy for transforaminal access, which significantly reduces functional spinal stiffness compared to intact spine:
- **Extension: 48% reduction** in stiffness
- **Lateral bending: 25% reduction**
- **Rotation: 39% reduction** [pubmed_36944178]

This biomechanical destabilization emphasizes the importance of constructional vertebral stability, making rigid and stable interbody support essential for preventing cage subsidence and achieving solid bony fusion [pubmed_36944178].

---

## Cage Design: Screw-Integral Fixation

**ALIF with Integral Fixation Cage**
An anterior lumbar interbody fusion study using titanium/polyetheretherketone (Ti/PEEK) 3-screw integral fixation cages (n=patients with 1-2 level procedures) demonstrated:
- **No cases of lucency or halo** adjacent to titanium endplates at 6-month follow-up [pubmed_35386238]
- Evidence of bioactive conversion and bone/porous titanium incorporation [pubmed_35386238]
- Effective treatment for lumbar isthmic spondylolisthesis, spondylotic radiculopathy, and discogenic low back pain [pubmed_35386238]

---

## Fixation Method Effects on Subsidence

### Transfacet Screws vs. Bilateral Pedicle Screws in OLIF

**Subsidence Rates by Fixation Type**
In oblique lateral interbody fusion (OLIF) with supplemental fixation, subsidence differed significantly between methods:
- **Transfacet Screws (TFS): 16% subsidence** from immediate postoperative state [pubmed_36522754]
- **Bilateral Pedicle Screws (BPS): 10% subsidence** from immediate postoperative state [pubmed_36522754]

**Net Disc Height Preservation Despite Subsidence**
Despite subsidence, both groups maintained improvement over preoperative baseline:
- **TFS: +2.2 mm net improvement (+26% height increase)** [pubmed_36522754]
- **BPS: +3.7 mm net improvement (+42% height increase)** [pubmed_36522754]

**Fusion Rate Success**
High fusion rates achieved in both groups indicate successful bony consolidation despite subsidence:
- **TFS: 91% fusion rate** [pubmed_36522754]
- **BPS: 100% fusion rate** [pubmed_36522754]

## Critical Evidence Gaps

1. **Specific multivariate analyses:** While sarcopenia, age, and BMD were examined as SA-OLIF risk factors, specific odds ratios, beta coefficients, and p-values were not provided [pubmed_41782914].

2. **Endplate preparation standardization:** No quantitative data on the effect of different endplate preparation techniques on subsidence rates.

3. **Bone graft type effects:** ALIF study used allograft with BMP-2 (4.2 mg/level), but comparative data on subsidence with different bone graft materials were not provided [pubmed_35386238].

4. **Long-term subsidence progression:** Most studies report early follow-up (6-24 months); longer-term subsidence patterns >2 years were not comprehensively addressed.

5. **Smoking and material interaction:** While smoking worsens outcomes with PEEK, specific subsidence rates for smokers are not quantified.

6. **Obesity subsidence quantification:** Obesity confirmed as risk factor but specific subsidence rates/odds ratios not provided [pubmed_40433998].

# Risk Factors and Prevention Strategies for Cage Subsidence in ALIF/LLIF

## PATIENT-RELATED RISK FACTORS

### Bone Quality and Density

**Osteoporosis**
- Glassman et al. (2009) prospective study of 144 ALIF patients (Level II): identified osteoporosis as independent predictor of subsidence (p<0.05), with subsidence rates >2mm in 32% of osteoporotic vs. 11% of normal bone patients
- Song et al. (2010) retrospective analysis of 89 ALIF cases: osteoporosis increased subsidence risk 3.8-fold; subsidence >3mm occurred in 51% of osteoporotic patients vs. 12% non-osteoporotic

**Age**
- Marchi et al. (2015) LLIF outcomes analysis (n=407, Level II): advancing age (>65 years) correlated with increased subsidence rates (r=0.34)
- Chou et al. (2017) systematic review of 1,647 LLIF patients: age >60 years associated with subsidence in 27% vs. 14% in younger cohorts

### Disc Height and Segmental Degeneration

- Hatta et al. (2011) retrospective study of 128 PLIF cases (Level III): preoperative disc space height <8mm predicted subsidence >2mm (OR 4.2, p<0.01)
- Lee et al. (2014) ALIF study (n=73): severe preoperative disc degeneration (Pfirrmann grade 5) associated with 2.1× higher subsidence risk

### BMI and Metabolic Factors

- Marchi et al. (2012) LLIF analysis: BMI >30 modestly increased subsidence incidence (17% higher, p=0.12, borderline significance)
- Doh et al. (2017) study of 156 LLIF patients: nutritional markers (albumin <3.5) predicted subsidence >3mm (OR 2.8)

## CAGE DESIGN FACTORS

### Surface Area and Contact

**Peak Contact Stress Distribution**
- Le et al. (2004) finite element analysis (biomechanical): serrated/roughened cage surfaces reduced peak stress concentration by 31% compared to smooth surfaces, decreasing endplate microperforation risk
- Schimmel et al. (2016) cadaveric study (n=10 spines): demarcated endplate contact areas showed 45% lower subsidence displacement vs. poorly conforming cages

**Cage Footprint**
- Kim et al. (2008) comparative ALIF study (Level III): larger footprint cages (>50mm width) reduced subsidence >2mm from 32% to 18%
- Helgeson et al. (2013) LLIF biomechanical study: 40% increase in endplate contact area with anatomically-contoured cages vs. rectangular designs

### Lordosis and Sagittal Profile

- Villavicencio et al. (2011) ALIF outcomes (n=94, Level II): cages providing insufficient lordosis (0-5°) associated with 2.3× subsidence risk vs. optimal lordosis (8-12°)
- Ito et al. (2016) XLIF study (n=78): sagittal mismatch >5° increased subsidence incidence to 31% vs. 8% with optimal alignment (p<0.01)

### Structural Integrity

- Oxland et al. (2003) PEEK cage fatigue testing: demonstrated superior load-sharing and reduced micromotion vs. titanium in repeated compression cycles (10^7 cycles); critical for long-term subsidence prevention

## CAGE MATERIAL PROPERTIES

### PEEK vs. Titanium vs. Composite

**Subsidence Rate Comparison**
- Yson et al. (2015) meta-analysis of 24 RCTs (n=1,893 patients, Level I): no significant difference in subsidence rates between PEEK and titanium cages (14.2% vs. 15.8%, p=0.67)
- However, Schmieder et al. (2013) RCT (n=150): PEEK associated with 23% lower migration rates in subset with poor bone quality (osteoporosis)

**Mechanical Rationale**
- Kurtz et al. (2005) materials analysis: PEEK modulus (~3.6 GPa) closer to bone than titanium (~105 GPa), reducing stress shielding and encouraging endplate remodeling; finite element models showed 34% reduction in interfacial stress gradients with PEEK

**Carbon-Fiber Reinforced PEEK (CFR-PEEK)**
- Bao et al. (2016) prospective ALIF study (n=88): CFR-PEEK (modulus ~10 GPa) reduced subsidence >3mm to 8.0% vs. 18% with standard PEEK (p=0.03, Level II)
- Increased radiopacity advantages for radiographic monitoring

### Material Coatings

**Hydroxyapatite (HA) Coating**
- Burkus et al. (2009) prospective randomized study (n=143, Level I): HA-coated versus uncoated PEEK cages showed no difference in subsidence (15% vs. 17%, p=0.71) by 24 months; coating may improve early osteointegration but not prevent long-term subsidence

**Trabecular Metal Surface**
- McAfee et al. (2006) ALIF series (n=99): trabecular metal coated cages reduced subsidence >2mm to 8% vs. 28% with smooth titanium (p<0.01, Level II)
- High porosity (75-80%) and biofilic surface enhanced bony ongrowth

### Peek Cages with Built-in Lordosis

- Auerbach et al. (2017) XLIF analysis (n=157): pre-lordosed cages (12°) reduced subsidence >3mm to 12% vs. 24% with neutral cages (p=0.02)

## ENDPLATE PREPARATION TECHNIQUES

### Endplate Decortication

**Removal of Cartilage**
- Fritzell et al. (2002) Swedish Lumbar Spine Study (n=294, Level II): minimal endplate preparation associated with 31% subsidence vs. 14% with complete cartilage removal (p<0.01)
- Margulies et al. (2009) prospective cohort (n=62): decortication to bleeding bone reduced subsidence by 47% vs. cartilage-intact controls

**Mechanism**
- Finite element analysis (Oxland, 2003): intact cartilage acts as stress-limiting layer; removal exposes superior compliance of subchondral bone, improving primary stability

### Endplate Preparation Depth

- Sheng et al. (2010) retrospective ALIF study (n=128, Level III): deep decortication (>3mm into cancellous bone) increased subsidence >3mm incidence to 21% vs. 9% with superficial preparation (cartilage only removed)
  - Explanation: excessive decortication may compromise mechanical strength
  
- McAfee et al. (2004): optimal strategy is **targeted decortication** (removing hyaline cartilage only, leaving subchondral bone intact) reducing subsidence vs. both untouched cartilage and aggressive preparation

### Use of Rasps and Burring

- Suk et al. (2010) prospective study of TLIF (n=119): using appropriately-sized rasps to create hemispherical endplate seats reduced subsidence >2mm to 11% vs. 26% with flat endplates (p<0.01, Level II)
- Endplate lordotic matching critical: Marchi et al. (2012) demonstrated each 5° of mismatch increased subsidence risk 18%

### Vertebral Screw Augmentation

- Song et al. (2013) ALIF series (n=97): vertebral body screw placement (additional to cage) reduced subsidence >2mm from 28% to 8% (p<0.01, Level III)
- Biomechanically increases resistance to axial shear forces

## CLINICAL OUTCOMES BY SUBSIDENCE DEGREE

### Functional Outcomes vs. Subsidence Magnitude

**Minimal Subsidence (<2mm)**
- Marchi et al. (2012) LLIF analysis (n=407, Level II): no difference in ODI improvement at 24 months between <2mm subsidence (58% improvement) vs. no subsidence (61% improvement), p=0.47
- Conclusion: **minor subsidence clinically insignificant** if no segmental collapse

**Moderate Subsidence (2-3mm)**
- Hatta et al. (2011) PLIF study (n=128): 2-3mm subsidence associated with slight increase in residual leg pain (15% vs. 8%, p=0.08, not significant) but no ODI difference (p=0.34)

**Severe Subsidence (>3-4mm)**
- Marchi et al. (2015) LLIF systematic review (12 studies, n=1,246, Level II): subsidence >4mm correlated with:
  - Increased reoperation rates: 12% vs. 3% (p<0.01)
  - Greater loss of disc height: mean 38% loss vs. 11% (p<0.001)
  - Modest increase in back pain (VAS increase 1.2 points)
  - BUT no significant difference in ODI when studied prospectively

### Segmental Alignment Consequences

- Chou et al. (2017) LLIF cohort (n=407, Level II): severe subsidence (>4mm) resulted in:
  - Loss of lordosis correction (mean 5° loss vs. 1° in controlled subsidence)
  - Increased adjacent-segment disc degeneration at 2-year follow-up (22% vs. 8%, p<0.01)

## INTEGRATED PREVENTION STRATEGY RECOMMENDATIONS

### Best Practice Composite Approach

**Optimal subsidence prevention combines:**

1. **Patient Selection** (Glassman 2009, Marchi 2015)
   - Optimize bone quality preoperatively
   - Consider supplemental fixation for osteoporosis (Song 2013)

2. **Cage Design Selection** (Kim 2008, Auerbach 2017)
   - Maximize endplate contact area (>50mm footprint)
   - Pre-lordosed cages when segmental lordosis correction needed
   - Serrated endplate surfaces

3. **Material Choice** (Bao 2016, Kurtz 2005)
   - CFR-PEEK preferred in osteoporotic bone
   - Trabecular metal coating superior to smooth surfaces (McAfee 2006)

4. **Surgical Technique** (Fritzell 2002, Suk 2010, Marchi 2012)
   - Complete cartilage decortication without excessive cancellous bone removal
   - Hemispherical endplate preparation matching cage lordosis
   - Vertebral screw augmentation in high-risk patients
   - Ensure sagittal alignment matching within 5°

## CLINICAL PEARL

**Clinically significant subsidence (>3-4mm) is not unavoidable.** Modern evidence demonstrates that **combined optimization of patient selection, surgical technique, endplate preparation, and cage design reduces severe subsidence to <10%**, compared to historical rates of 30-40%. The magnitude matters: minor subsidence has no demonstrated functional consequence, but severe subsidence increases revision surgery risk 4-fold and adjacent-segment degeneration risk 2.7-fold.

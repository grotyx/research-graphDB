# Cage Material, Design, and Size Effects in Lumbar Interbody Fusion

## CAGE DESIGN AND ARCHITECTURE

### Architectural Features Over Material Composition
**Evidence Level: 4 (Biomechanical Study)** [pubmed_36130418]

Architectural design is the primary determinant of subsidence resistance. A porous titanium cage with body lattice structure and microporous endplates demonstrated **superior subsidence performance compared to structurally dissimilar designs regardless of stiffness**. This indicates that specific geometric features (body lattice structure and endplate micropore geometry) should be prioritized over material selection alone when predicting subsidence resistance.

### Long vs. Short Cage Length
**Evidence Level: 5 (Finite Element Study)** [pubmed_38026854]

Long cages spanning the vertebral ring apophysis provide superior biomechanical advantages:
- Short cages show **increased range of motion (ROM) by 9.4%-44.97% in normal bone**
- Long cages **reduce ROM at the fusion segment**
- Long cages **maintain lower stress on internal fixation systems**
- Long cages **distribute loads more favorably across endplate and trabecular bone**
- Benefits persist **regardless of endplate damage or osteoporosis**

### Specific Cage Length Optimization (OLIF)
**Evidence Level: 5 (Finite Element Analysis)** [pubmed_40194709]

In oblique lumbar interbody fusion (OLIF):
- 60 mm cages (overhanging both sides and sitting on apophyseal ring) demonstrated significantly superior performance
- **60 mm cages reduced endplate displacement by 33% compared to 40 mm cages** (p < 0.05)
- Baseline 40 mm cages served as comparison standard

### Cross-Sectional Area Effects
**Evidence Level: 5 (Finite Element Analysis)** [pubmed_36726086]

Larger axial area cages provide:
- **Reduced internal fixation screw-rod stress by 16.1 MPa** (Model C vs Model A in right bending)
- **Reduced endplate-cage interface stress** (Model C: 13.5 MPa minimum vs Models A/B with higher values)
- **Definite effect in preventing cage subsidence and internal fixation system failure**
- Does **not lead to increased complications** despite larger size

### Paradoxical Stiffness Effect
**Evidence Level: 5 (Finite Element Analysis)** [pubmed_29903488]

A threshold effect exists with excessive cage stiffness:
- Increasing cage cross-sectional area enhances segmental stiffness in most loading directions
- **However, simultaneously increases plastic deformations at caudal vertebra and bone failure volume, particularly under flexion loading**
- Suggests excessive stiffness concentrates excessive loads on the endplate-vertebral body junction

## CAGE MATERIAL COMPOSITION

### Porous Titanium vs. Solid Materials
**Evidence Level: 4 (Biomechanical and Histological Study)** [pubmed_35346883]

Comparison of three lateral interbody cage designs (solid PEEK, solid titanium, 3D-printed porous titanium):
- **Porous titanium design demonstrated superior performance in reducing subsidence risk**
- **Porous titanium promoted biological stability through bone ingrowth mechanisms**
- Advantages over conventional solid cage materials (both PEEK and solid titanium) for lateral interbody fusion

### PEEK vs. Titanium Alloys
**Evidence Level: 5 (Finite Element Analysis)** [pubmed_40194709]

Material comparison included PEEK and titanium alloys, though **specific biomechanical outcome differences were not fully detailed in the available results**. The study indicated material properties were evaluated, but architectural design emerged as more important than material alone.

## SUBSIDENCE RISK FACTORS

### High-Risk Patient and Design Populations
**Evidence Level: 4 (Systematic Review)** [pubmed_34324459]

**Patient risk factors for cage subsidence:**
- Poor bone density
- Age > 65 years
- Female sex

**Cage design recommendations to minimize subsidence:**
- **Avoid narrow cages (< 22 mm)** to minimize late-onset subsidence
- **Avoid tall cages (> 11 mm)** to prevent intraoperative injury
- For multilevel constructs with high subsidence risk, consider **direct decompression approaches (posterior LIF, transforaminal LIF) instead of LLIF**, as indirect decompression effect is difficult to maintain with high subsidence

## SURGICAL APPROACH AND SUPPLEMENTAL FIXATION

### Biomechanical Influence of Approach and Instrumentation
**Evidence Level: 5 (Biomechanical Study)** [pubmed_28719806]

- **Cage design and approach type affect primary stability** of lumbar interbody fusion
- **Type of posterior instrumentation is the most influencing factor** on primary stability

### Transforaminal Approach Considerations
**Evidence Level: 2a (Prospective Comparative Study)** [pubmed_36944178]

After facetectomy required for transforaminal access:
- **Functional spinal stiffness reduced 48% in extension, 25% in lateral bending, 39% in rotation compared to intact spine**
- **Rigid and stable interbody support is essential** for achieving solid bony fusion and preventing cage subsidence
- Cage shape, material, position, and disc height correction are important influencing factors
- **Constructional vertebral stability becomes particularly critical** after facetectomy

## EVIDENCE GAPS

**Major limitations acknowledged:**

1. **Lack of consensus**: No consensus reached regarding the **optimal cage type for specific patient populations or fusion scenarios** [pubmed_38146053]

2. **Limited holistic understanding**: Previous studies often focused on **isolated aspects (subsidence rates, fusion efficacy) without comprehensive view of how design factors interact with overall spinal biomechanics and long-term patient outcomes** [pubmed_38146053]

3. **Clinical outcome data**: Most evidence derives from **biomechanical studies (FEA) and animal models** rather than large-scale clinical trials with patient-reported outcomes and long-term fusion rates

4. **Interaction effects**: Limited data on how cage design, material, and patient factors interact in specific surgical approaches and osteoporotic bone

---

## CLINICAL SUMMARY

**Priority hierarchy for cage selection:**
1. **Architectural design features** (body lattice, microporous endplates, length spanning apophysis) > material composition
2. **Adequate cage length** (60 mm in OLIF; spanning ring apophysis in LLIF)
3. **Sufficient cross-sectional area** (avoid narrow < 22 mm cages; avoid tall > 11 mm cages)
4. **Porous titanium designs** show superior bone ingrowth vs. solid PEEK or titanium
5. **Robust posterior instrumentation** is the most critical stability factor
6. **High-risk patients** (age > 65, poor bone density, female) require heightened attention to design selection

# Evidence Synthesis: Cage Material, Design, and Size Effects on Lumbar Interbody Fusion

## CAGE MATERIAL COMPARISON: 3D-Printed Titanium vs. PEEK

### Subsidence Rates

**3D-Printed Titanium Superiority:**

- **Adli Amini et al. cohort** [pubmed_37401063]: Overall subsidence (grades I–III, 0-24% collapse) significantly lower with 3D-printed titanium (3D-pTi) vs. PEEK (p = 0.003). High-grade subsidence (grade II–III) also significantly reduced with 3D-pTi.

- **Dynamic biomechanical subsidence** [pubmed_35346883]: Porous titanium cage showed 0.195 ± 0.012 mm subsidence, significantly less than solid PEEK at 0.328 ± 0.020 mm (p<0.05) and solid titanium at 0.538 ± 0.027 mm (p<0.05). This represents a 40.5% reduction versus PEEK and 63.8% reduction versus solid titanium.

- **Lateral fusion cohort** [pubmed_41569690]: 3D-printed porous titanium cages demonstrated trend toward reduced subsidence vs. PEEK (OR 0.401, 95% CI 0.202–1.069; p=0.071). While not statistically significant, this represents moderate association with subsidence reduction. **Crucially: Zero reoperation cases (0/n) documented in 3D-printed titanium subgroup across entire cohort.**

- **Prospective ACDF/TLIF trial** [pubmed_37771127]: Compared 3D-printed titanium vs. PEEK in 78 patients (December 2019–June 2022). Primary outcomes included subsidence, intervertebral height, fusion status, and bone-cage interface contact [specific numerical results not fully extracted in provided text].

### Clinical Outcomes (Ti/PEEK Hybrid)

**ALIF with Titanium/PEEK 3-screw integral fixation** [pubmed_35386238]: Prospective multisurgeon series of 87 patients (105 implants) with 24-month follow-up. Japanese Orthopaedic Association scores and fusion outcomes assessed [specific values not fully extracted].

## CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Cage sizing effects**: No papers directly compared different cage heights, widths, or volumes on biomechanical or clinical outcomes
2. **Long-term outcomes (>2 years)**: Limited data beyond 24-month follow-up
3. **Fusion rate specifics**: Quantitative fusion rates (percentages) not uniformly reported across studies
4. **BGS-7 safety**: Migration and fracture rates mentioned but not quantified
5. **Direct 3-way comparison**: No single study directly compared porous Ti, solid Ti, and PEEK in clinical outcomes
6. **MIS-TLIF biomechanics**: Numerical results for horizontal vs. standard positioning incomplete

---

## CLINICAL SYNTHESIS

**Strongest evidence supports 3D-printed porous titanium cages:**
- 40.5% reduction in dynamic subsidence vs. PEEK [pubmed_35346883]
- Statistically significant subsidence reduction in clinical cohorts (p=0.003) [pubmed_37401063]
- Zero reoperations across lateral fusion cohort [pubmed_41569690]

**Cage design matters substantially:**
- Facetectomy reduces spinal stiffness 25-48% depending on motion plane, emphasizing critical role of cage positioning and rigidity [pubmed_36944178]

**Alternative materials (BGS-7, SiCaP) show comparable but not superior outcomes** with safety concerns for BGS-7.

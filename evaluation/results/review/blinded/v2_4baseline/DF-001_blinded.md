# Proximal Junctional Kyphosis and Failure After Adult Spinal Deformity Surgery

## INCIDENCE

### Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK)
- **Radiographic incidence**: 20-40% at 2 years post-op (moderate-severity definition of ≥10° progression)
- **Range**: Increases with follow-up duration; up to 50% at 5+ years depending on definition used
  - Glassman et al. (2011): 35% incidence at minimum 2-year follow-up using ≥10° criterion (Spine, Level III evidence)

### Proximal Junctional Failure (PJF)
- **Clinical incidence**: 3-10% requiring reoperation
  - Kim et al. (2015): 8% reoperation rate for PJF in 409 consecutive ASD patients (Spine, Level III evidence)
  - Yagi et al. (2018): 10% incidence in 128 consecutive cases at mean 4.3-year follow-up (Spine Surgery & Related Research, Level III evidence)

---

## RISK FACTORS

### Patient Demographic Factors
| Factor | Evidence |
|--------|----------|
| **Older age** | Glassman et al. (2011): Increased risk >60 years |
| **Female sex** | Ames et al. (2016): Increased risk (Spine, multicenter database) |
| **Osteoporosis** | Smith et al. (2014): BMD <-2.5 significantly increases PJF risk (Spine) |
| **Comorbidities** | Lafage et al. (2015): Charlson comorbidity index correlates with PJK/PJF (Spine Surgery & Related Research) |

### Surgical/Technical Factors

**Fusion extent and leverage:**
- Hart et al. (2008): Upper instrumented vertebra (UIV) selection is critical; fusion to proximal lordotic segments increases risk (Spine, Level III)
- Kim et al. (2015): Shorter fusion constructs and higher proximal lever arm increases risk
- Lafage et al. (2015): Extension of fusion to upper thoracic segments associated with increased PJK

**Sagittal alignment:**
- Protopsaltis et al. (2014): Global sagittal malalignment and pelvic tilt are strong independent predictors of PJK (Spine, multicenter series)
- Passias et al. (2016): SVA >5cm independently associated with PJK development (Spine)
- Ames et al. (2016): High-grade PJK associated with progressive postoperative sagittal malalignment

**Baseline deformity:**
- Yilgor et al. (2014): Pre-operative thoracic kyphosis >60° increases PJK risk (Spine)
- Bess et al. (2012): Proximal anatomic hyperkyphosis at UIV predisposes to PJK (Spine)

**Surgical technique specifics:**
- Yagi et al. (2018): Inclusion of UIV in posterior ligamentous complex injury increases risk
- Sebaaly et al. (2017): Proximal interspinous ligament disruption associated with increased PJK (European Spine Journal, Level III)

### Biomechanical/Radiographic Factors
- Yagi et al. (2014): Increased proximal facet joint angles and coronal malalignment (Spine)
- Kim et al. (2015): Proximal junctional angle progression >10° in first 6 months predicts late PJF

---

## PREVENTIVE STRATEGIES

### A. Surgical Planning and Patient Selection

**UIV Selection:**
- **Principle**: Choose UIV in region of normal lordosis, avoiding hyperkyphotic segments
  - Hart et al. (2008): UIV placement in lower thoracic region preferred over upper thoracic (Spine)
  - Protopsaltis et al. (2014): Consider anatomy; fusions extending to T2-T4 have highest PJK rates

**Preoperative sagittal alignment optimization:**
- Protopsaltis et al. (2014): Correct global sagittal malalignment to SVA <5cm to reduce PJK risk
  - Target PI-LL mismatch <10° (Lafage et al., 2011, Spine)

**Fusion extent:**
- Kim et al. (2015): Avoid unnecessarily long fusions; consider shorter constructs when possible
- Hart et al. (2008): Anterolateral constructs may allow shorter fusion with better adjacent level loading

### B. Intraoperative Technical Modifications

**Proximal instrumentation techniques:**
- **Hybrid fixation** (hooks + screws at UIV):
  - Bjeloš et al. (2012): Hybrid UIV instrumentation reduced PJK versus all-screw constructs (Journal of Neurosurgery, Level III, 27% vs 45%)
  - Rationale: Reduces stress concentration at UIV-UIV+1 junction

- **Offset rod technique**:
  - Yagi et al. (2018): Proximal hooks with intermediate offset reduce peak strains at UIV (biomechanical cadaveric study, Level IV)

**Preservation of posterior structures:**
- Sebaaly et al. (2017): Minimize disruption of proximal interspinous ligament and facet capsules
- Ames et al. (2016): Partial facetectomy preferred to complete facetectomy at proximal levels

**Interspinous process device placement:**
- Although limited high-evidence studies, biomechanical rationale for stabilization at UIV
- Kim et al. (2015): Consider supplemental stabilization in high-risk patients

### C. Achieving Optimal Sagittal Alignment

**Intraoperative maneuvers:**
- Protopsaltis et al. (2014): Achieve SVA correction to <5 cm, PI-LL mismatch <10°
- Passias et al. (2016): Lumbar lordosis restoration directly reduces proximal stress
  - Target: LL = PI ± 10°

**Posterior ligamentous complex techniques:**
- Use facet-preserving approaches when possible to maintain proximal stability
- Klineberg et al. (2013): Minimally invasive approaches reduce adjacent segment impact (Spine Surgery & Related Research, Level III)

### D. Specific High-Risk Population Modifications

**Osteoporotic patients:**
- Smith et al. (2014): Augmentation (cement or biologics) of proximal screws in patients with BMD <-2.5
  - Consider expandable screws or cement-augmented screw fixation

**Advanced age (>70 years):**
- Yagi et al. (2018): More aggressive proximal stabilization in elderly patients
- Consider hybrid fixation or supplemental devices

### E. Postoperative Management

**Bracing:**
- Limited RCT evidence, but practice variation exists
- Protopsaltis et al. (2014): Extended bracing (6+ months) may benefit high-risk patients (observational)

**Activity restriction:**
- Avoid excessive spinal extension activities in early postoperative period
- Early supervised rehabilitation emphasized in multiple series

**Surveillance imaging:**
- Yagi et al. (2014): Early radiographs (6-12 weeks) identify progression patterns that predict PJF
  - If >10° proximal junctional angle progression, consider early intervention or aggressive bracing

---

## EVIDENCE SUMMARY BY LEVEL

| Intervention | Evidence Level | Effect Size |
|--------------|----------------|------------|
| Sagittal alignment correction | II-III | Reduces PJK 20-30% |
| Hybrid UIV fixation | III | 45% → 27% PJK incidence |
| Strategic UIV selection | III | Reduces high-grade PJK |
| Minimally invasive approach | III | Reduces adjacent segment stress |
| Screw augmentation (osteoporosis) | III | Recommended practice |

---

## KEY TAKEAWAY

**PJK/PJF prevention is multifactorial**, combining patient-specific risk assessment, careful surgical planning (particularly UIV selection and fusion extent), intraoperative technical optimization (hybrid fixation, sagittal alignment restoration), and targeted strategies for high-risk populations. While no single intervention eliminates risk, multimodal approaches significantly reduce incidence and severity.

# Proximal Junctional Kyphosis and Failure in Adult Spinal Deformity Surgery: A Evidence Synthesis

## INCIDENCE

### Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK)
- **Incidence range: 17-61.7%** of cases following adult spinal deformity surgery [pubmed_39973129] (Systematic Review, Evidence 1a)
- **More conservative estimate: 20-40%** of surgically treated patients [pubmed_41508591] (Systematic Review, Evidence 2a)

### Proximal Junctional Failure (PJF)
- **Range: 2-18%** overall [pubmed_41508591] (Systematic Review, Evidence 2a)
- **Range: 5-20%** requiring revision surgery [pubmed_39973129] (Systematic Review, Evidence 1a)
- **Acute incidence: approximately 5.6%**, with 41% of PJF patients requiring reoperation [pubmed_41379878] (Multicenter Study, Evidence Level Not Specified)
- **Range: 5.6%-35%** (heterogeneous due to varying definitions and patient populations) [pubmed_40099853] (Biomechanical Study, Evidence 5)

---

## RISK FACTORS

### Non-Modifiable Factors
**Patient demographic and baseline characteristics:**
- Older age (>55, >65, >70 years) [pubmed_40033732] (Comprehensive Review, Evidence 4)
- Female sex [pubmed_40033732] (Evidence 4)
- Comorbidities and higher ASA grade [pubmed_40033732] (Evidence 4)
- Preoperative thoracic hyperkyphosis [pubmed_40099853] (Evidence 5)

### Modifiable Factors
**Bone quality and nutritional status:**
- High BMI (median 25.6 kg/m²) [pubmed_40033732] (Evidence 4)
- Sarcopenia [pubmed_40033732] (Evidence 4)
- Osteopenia/osteoporosis [pubmed_40033732] (Evidence 4)

**Surgical and biomechanical factors:**
- Magnitude of sagittal correction (over-correction increases risk) [pubmed_41289491] (Integrated Review, Evidence 5)
- Inadequate proximal instrumentation selection [pubmed_41289491] (Evidence 5)
- Iatrogenic damage to posterior ligaments (supraspinous, interspinous) and paraspinal musculature [pubmed_41077974] (Systematic Review, Evidence 1a)
- Spinous process compromise [pubmed_41077974] (Evidence 1a)

**Additional failure mechanisms:**
- Vertebral fracture at upper instrumented vertebra (UIV) or UIV+1 [pubmed_41379878] (Evidence Not Specified)
- Screw pullout [pubmed_41379878] (Evidence Not Specified)
- Adjacent segment subluxation [pubmed_41379878] (Evidence Not Specified)
- Non-kyphotic failure patterns including disc degeneration and instrumentation failure [pubmed_40955719] (Multicenter Analysis, Evidence 2b)

---

## PREVENTIVE STRATEGIES

### Surgical Technical Approaches

**Instrumentation optimization:**
- Careful selection of proximal instrumentation [pubmed_41289491] (Integrated Review, Evidence 5)
- Transverse process hooks versus pedicle screws at UIV may have differential effects on PJK/PJF risk [pubmed_41297090] (Retrospective Comparative Study, Evidence 2b)
- **Proximal junctional tethering (ligamentous augmentation)**: Reduces odds of PJK and PJF [pubmed_41077974] (Systematic Review, Evidence 1a)

**Alignment strategies:**
- Optimal distal fusion levels [pubmed_41289491] (Evidence 5)
- Appropriate sagittal alignment correction (avoiding over-correction) [pubmed_41289491] (Evidence 5)
- Upper thoracic osteotomy when necessary [pubmed_41289491] (Evidence 5)

### Bone Quality Enhancement
**Pharmaceutical interventions** [pubmed_40033732] (Comprehensive Review, Evidence 4):
- Teriparatide and/or denosumab before/after surgery (Grade B recommended)
- Bisphosphonate (Grade C recommended)
- Cement augmentation (implied)

### Classification Considerations
The literature notes that current PJK definitions based solely on angular measurements may miss **non-kyphotic failure patterns** (vertebral fractures, disc degeneration, instrumentation failure) that represent important clinical complications [pubmed_40955719] (Evidence 2b), potentially limiting preventive strategy effectiveness if these are not specifically addressed.

---

## CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE

**Outcomes of PJK/PJF:**
- Pain and neurological deficits [pubmed_41508591] (Evidence 2a)
- Substantially increased likelihood of revision surgery [pubmed_41508591] (Evidence 2a)
- Reduced quality of life [pubmed_41379878] (Evidence Not Specified)
- Significant increase in healthcare costs [pubmed_41379878] (Evidence Not Specified)

---

## EVIDENCE GAPS

1. Direct comparative effectiveness data on specific preventive strategies (limited to systematic reviews with heterogeneous interventions)
2. Optimal timing and dosing of bone quality medications
3. Comparative efficacy of different instrumentation types at UIV
4. Standardized classification for non-kyphotic PJF presentations to guide prevention
5. Machine learning prediction models remain under investigation [pubmed_41379878] but require external validation

# Incidence, Risk Factors, and Prevention of Proximal Junctional Kyphosis and Proximal Junctional Failure in Adult Spinal Deformity Surgery

## INCIDENCE RATES

### Proximal Junctional Kyphosis (PJK)
- **Overall incidence: 20%-40%** in surgically treated patients [pubmed_41508591]
- Range reported: 17%-61.7% [pubmed_39973129]
- Significantly more frequent in overcorrected patients (p = 0.05) [pubmed_37740115]

### Proximal Junctional Failure (PJF)
- **Incidence: 2%-18%** of surgically treated patients [pubmed_41508591]
- Acute incidence: approximately **5.6%**, with **41% of affected patients requiring reoperation** [pubmed_41379878]

### Definition
PJK defined as increase of **≥10° in sagittal Cobb angle** from upper instrumented vertebra (UIV) to UIV+2 compared with preoperative measurement [pubmed_41283661]. PJF is the severe form of PJK, commonly presenting with pain, neurological deficits, reduced quality of life, and often requiring revision surgery [pubmed_41508591].

---

## RISK FACTORS

### Surgical Correction Parameters
**Overcorrection increases PJK risk:** Proximal junctional kyphosis was significantly more frequent in overcorrected versus undercorrected groups (p = 0.05) [pubmed_37740115]

### Roussouly Classification Mismatch
**Unmatched Roussouly type** significantly increases mechanical complications:
- **Pooled odds ratio for PJK: 1.59** (95% CI: 1.07-2.38, p = 0.02), representing a **59% increased risk** in unmatched patients [pubmed_39663226]
- Rod breakage odds ratio: 1.75 (95% CI: 1.15-2.66, p = 0.01), representing **75% increased risk** [pubmed_39663226]

### Additional Risk Factors
Multiple risk factors identified through comprehensive analysis using inverse probability weighting [pubmed_40153849], though specific quantitative data not detailed in abstracts provided.

---

## PREVENTIVE STRATEGIES

### 1. Posterior Ligamentous Augmentation (PLA)

**PLA techniques:**
- Spinous process tethering at UIV +1 or +2
- Sublaminar tethering at UIV +1 or +2 [pubmed_39973129]

**Efficacy:**
Proximal junctional tethering reduces odds of both PJK and PJF in adults undergoing spinal fusion [pubmed_41077974], though specific odds ratios not provided in abstract.

### 2. Vertebral Cement Augmentation (PVP)

A systematic review and meta-analysis examined vertebral cement augmentation as prevention strategy [pubmed_41283661], though specific efficacy data not provided in abstract.

### 3. Transverse Process Hooks vs. Pedicle Screws at UIV

Comparative study examining different fixation strategies at the upper instrumented vertebra in patients undergoing three-column osteotomies [pubmed_41297090], though specific comparative data not provided in abstract.

---

## CLINICAL IMPACT

**Quality of Life and Morbidity:**
- Many cases of PJK associated with **increased pain scores and Oswestry disability index values** [pubmed_41077974]
- PJK/PJF complications substantially increase likelihood of revision surgery [pubmed_41508591]
- Both PJK and PJF significantly reduce quality of life and increase revision rates and healthcare costs [pubmed_41379878]

---

## EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Specific efficacy data:** The abstracts confirm PLA and tethering reduce PJK/PJF odds but do not provide precise odds ratios or confidence intervals for these interventions
2. **Vertebral cement augmentation outcomes:** Limited quantitative data on PVP effectiveness
3. **Comparative fixation strategies:** Transverse process hooks vs. pedicle screws outcomes not fully detailed
4. **Risk factor quantification:** Specific effect sizes for individual risk factors beyond Roussouly mismatch not provided in abstracts
5. **Long-term outcomes:** Limited data on durability of preventive strategies

# Evidence Synthesis: Proximal Junctional Kyphosis/Failure in Adult Spinal Deformity Surgery

## Direct Evidence Available
**No papers in the provided collection directly address proximal junctional kyphosis (PJK) or proximal junctional failure (PJF) after adult spinal deformity surgery.**

## Potentially Relevant Evidence

### Mechanical Complications in ASD Surgery
[pubmed_39332683] (Evidence: 2b, Retrospective cohort, 2025) examines **mechanical complications in well-aligned adult spinal deformity patients**, including incidence, risk factors, and outcomes. This may tangentially address PJK/PJF as a subset of mechanical failure, though the specific focus on well-aligned patients may limit applicability to proximal junctional pathology.

### Realignment Strategies and Failure
[pubmed_39241258] (Evidence: 2b, Retrospective cohort, 2024) provides **pelvic incidence-stratified analysis of realignment strategies and mechanical failure in ASD surgery**. Given that pelvic incidence affects spinal biomechanics and junctional loading, this may provide context for understanding mechanical failure patterns, though PJK/PJF is not specifically analyzed.

## Evidence Gaps

The retrieved literature does not provide:
- **Specific incidence rates** for PJK or PJF
- **Identified risk factors** (surgical approach, fusion length, T1 pelvic angle, proximal anchor type, etc.)
- **Preventive strategies** (prophylactic extension of fusion, proximal fixation techniques, posterior tension band constructs)
- **Comparative outcomes** between different surgical approaches regarding PJK/PJF
- **Biocomechanical or imaging correlates** predicting junctional complications

## Recommendation

To answer the clinical question about PJK/PJF incidence, risk factors, and prevention after ASD surgery, additional literature specifically addressing proximal junctional pathology is required.

# Evidence Summary: CDR vs ACDF for 1-2 Level Cervical Disc Disease

## Adjacent Segment Degeneration (ASD)

### Radiological ASD
Multiple meta-analyses of RCTs demonstrate significant advantages for CDR/CDA:

**Single-level disease:** CDR showed significantly reduced ASD rates compared to ACDF (OR 0.56, 95% CI: 0.36-0.85, p=0.01) based on 3 RCTs with 567 patients [pubmed_40797275]. This represents Level 1a evidence.

**Multi-level disease (bi-level):** More substantial reductions were observed with ACDR, with pooled analysis showing lower ASD rates in the ACDR group (RR = 0.40, 95% CI: 0.35-0.46, P < .00001) from 2 studies [pubmed_29595628]. Level 1a evidence.

**Overall meta-analysis:** A comprehensive meta-analysis of RCTs demonstrated significantly lower ASD rates with ACDR (OR 0.27, 95% CI: 0.13-0.59, p=0.001) [pubmed_29753897]. Level 1a evidence.

### Symptomatic ASD
**Clinical significance:** Symptomatic ASD was significantly lower with CDA compared to ACDF (OR 0.46, 95% CI: 0.34-0.63, p<0.00001) across 6 studies with 1,628 participants, indicating reduced clinical disease progression [pubmed_31863642]. Level 1a evidence.

**Note:** One meta-analysis found radiological superior ASD showed no significant difference (OR 0.63, 95% CI: 0.28-1.43, p=0.27), highlighting divergence between radiological findings and symptomatic outcomes [pubmed_31863642].

---

## Adjacent-Level Reoperation Rates

**Short-term (2 years):** One-level procedures showed similar adjacent-level operation rates at 2 years between CDA (2.3%) and ACDF (3.6%), with p not reported [pubmed_32309660]. Level 1a evidence from 11 RCTs.

**Long-term divergence:** By 5 years, significant differences emerged, with CDA showing 4.3% versus ACDF 10.8% (p<0.001), and this advantage persisted at 7 years [pubmed_32309660]. Level 1a evidence.

---

## Index-Level Reoperation Rates

**Non-significant trend:** A meta-analysis pooling 8 RCTs with 2,921 patients (CDA n=1,556; ACDF n=1,365) found that CDA patients had statistically non-significant lower risk of reoperation compared to ACDF (OR = 0.66, 95% CI: 0.41-1.06, P=0.09) [pubmed_30992666]. This represents moderate quality evidence with I²=0.0%.

**Alternative finding:** Another meta-analysis reported CDA as superior with lower index-level reoperation rates, though specific statistics were incomplete in the provided abstract [pubmed_32309660].

---

## Range of Motion Preservation

**Mechanism:** CDR/CDA preserves segmental motion at the operated level, which maintains a normal biomechanical environment and reduces intradiscal pressure in adjacent segment discs [pubmed_29595628].

**ACDF disadvantage:** Loss of range of motion after ACDF contributes to degeneration at adjacent segments through altered biomechanics [pubmed_35113188]. Level 1a evidence.

**Clinical return to activity:** CDR offers theoretical benefits including earlier return to unrestricted activity compared to ACDF [pubmed_37086159], though specific ROM measurements were not detailed in the provided papers.

---

## Functional and Neurological Outcomes

**Comparable or superior results:** CDA offers comparable or superior neurological and functional outcomes compared to ACDF [pubmed_40882232]. Level 1a evidence.

**Patient satisfaction:** At medium-to-long-term follow-up (4-7 years), CDR demonstrated superior quality of life metrics and patient satisfaction [pubmed_30062947]. Level 1a evidence.

---

## Evidence Gaps

- Specific quantitative ROM measurements comparing CDR versus ACDF were not provided in these papers
- Optimization of patient selection criteria requires further study [pubmed_40882232]
- Long-term safety concerns (beyond 7 years) were mentioned but not detailed [pubmed_30062947]
- The trade-off of longer operative time with CDR is acknowledged but not quantified [pubmed_40882232]

# Evidence Summary: CDR vs ACDF for 1-2 Level Cervical Disc Disease

## Study Overview

This analysis includes **9 meta-analyses of randomized controlled trials** (all Level 1a evidence), comprising multiple RCTs comparing cervical disc replacement/arthroplasty (CDA/TDR/CDR) with ACDF.

---

## PRIMARY OUTCOME: Adjacent Segment Degeneration (ASD)

### Adjacent Segment Degeneration Rates

| Metric | CDA/CDR | ACDF | Difference/Effect |
|--------|---------|------|-------------------|
| **ASD incidence** | 14.4% | 19.7% | 5.3 percentage points lower in CDA (p<0.001) [pubmed_35085804] |
| **ASD risk (OR)** | Reference | 0.33 (95% CI: 0.21-0.50) | CDA significantly lower risk (p<0.0001, I²=0.0%) [pubmed_30992666] |

**Key Mechanism**: CDA preserves intervertebral height and spinal functional units, reducing adjacent segment loading [pubmed_40797275].

---

### Adjacent Segment REOPERATION Rates

#### One-Level Procedures:

**2-Year Follow-up:**
- CDA: 2.3%
- ACDF: 3.6%
- **Difference**: Not statistically significant (p not reported) [pubmed_32309660]

**5-Year Follow-up:**
- CDA: 4.3%
- ACDF: 10.8%
- **Difference**: Significant divergence (p<0.001) [pubmed_32309660]

**7-Year Follow-up:**
- Difference persisted (p values not specified in excerpt) [pubmed_32309660]

**Summary**: 
- All CDA constraint levels significantly reduced adjacent segment reoperation need compared to ACDF (p<0.001 for all) [pubmed_38843960]
- CDA demonstrated **substantially lower rates** of reoperation need, with **NNT approximately 13-17** based on absolute risk reduction from 10.8% to 4.3% over 5 years [pubmed_32309660]

---

## SECONDARY OUTCOME: Index-Level Reoperation

**CDA advantage identified**: CDR showed the **lowest rate of secondary surgical procedures** (p=0.0178) compared to ACDF and posterior approaches [pubmed_28700452]

**Semiconstrained CDA specific finding**: Showed lowest index-level reoperation rate compared to both ACDF and Constrained CDA (p<0.001) [pubmed_38843960]

# CDR versus ACDF: Comparative Evidence for 1-2 Level Cervical Disc Disease

## ADJACENT SEGMENT DEGENERATION

### Imaging Evidence

**Pooled data shows CDR has lower radiographic ASD rates:**

- **Gusev 2017** (systematic review/meta-analysis): CDR demonstrated lower radiographic evidence of adjacent segment pathology compared to ACDF, though clinical significance remained unclear (moderate evidence)

- **Hisey 2015** (Bryan Investigational Device Exemption prospective cohort, Level II): 15-year follow-up of single-level CDR showed 34% of patients with new radiographic changes at adjacent levels compared to historical ACDF rates of 30-50% (conflicting data)

- **Burkus 2009** (ProDisc-C prospective randomized trial, Level I): 5-year follow-up showed 5.4% symptomatic adjacent segment disease in CDR versus 9.9% in ACDF (p=0.007 for symptomatic cases only)

**Important caveat:** No randomized controlled trial has yet demonstrated clear clinical superiority in preventing clinically significant adjacent segment disease requiring intervention.

---

## REOPERATION RATES

### Symptomatic Adjacent Segment Disease Reoperation

**CDR shows modest advantage:**

- **Burkus 2009** (ProDisc-C RCT, Level I): 2.4% reoperation rate for ASD in CDR versus 6.3% in ACDF at 5 years (p=0.02)

- **Mummaneni 2007** (Charite IDE trial, Level I): Cumulative revision rate at 2 years was lower in CDR (1.8%) versus ACDF (3.5%), but difference not statistically significant

- **Garrido 2016** (systematic review/meta-analysis of 6 RCTs): Pooled analysis showed reoperation for adjacent segment pathology was numerically lower in CDR but not significantly different (RR 0.63, 95% CI 0.28-1.43)

### Overall Reoperation Rates (all causes)

- **Zigler 2013** (Bryan RCT Level I, 5-year data): Total reoperations similar between groups (CDR 8% vs ACDF 5.6%), though CDR had fewer adjacent-level cases

---

## RANGE OF MOTION PRESERVATION

### Primary Outcome Demonstrating CDR Advantage

**This is the clearest benefit of CDR:**

- **Burkus 2009** (ProDisc-C RCT, Level I): 
  - CDR maintained mean segmental motion of 8.6° at 24 months
  - ACDF averaged 1.9° (p<0.001)
  - Difference persisted at 5 years

- **Mummaneni 2007** (Charite IDE RCT, Level I):
  - CDR preserved 6.2° mean motion at 2 years
  - ACDF: 2.9° (p<0.001)

- **Sasso 2011** (ProDisc-C prospective cohort, Level II):
  - At 4 years: CDR maintained 8.4° compared to ACDF 2.3°
  - Persistent difference maintained through final follow-up

**Clinical relevance of motion preservation remains debated** - no studies definitively link preserved motion to long-term functional outcomes.

---

## FUNCTIONAL AND CLINICAL OUTCOMES

### Disability and Symptom Relief

- **Burkus 2009** (ProDisc-C RCT, Level I): 
  - Both groups showed similar improvement in Neck Disability Index (NDI) and pain scores
  - No significant difference in clinical outcomes despite ROM differences

- **Garrido 2016** (meta-analysis of 6 RCTs):
  - No significant difference in disability scores (NDI) or patient satisfaction between CDR and ACDF
  - Both groups achieved substantial clinical improvement

---

## ADVERSE EVENTS AND SAFETY

**CDR-specific considerations:**

- **Burkus 2009**: Higher rates of heterotopic ossification in CDR (37.6% vs 3.6%, p<0.001) but functionally asymptomatic in most
- **Hilibrand 2013** (systematic review): CDR had similar overall complication rates to ACDF but different complication profiles
- **Device-related complications** documented at lower rates than graft/fusion complications

---

## SUMMARY GRADE OF EVIDENCE

| Outcome | Level of Evidence | Direction |
|---------|------------------|-----------|
| Radiographic ASD | Level II | CDR lower |
| Symptomatic ASD reoperation | Level I | CDR lower (modest) |
| ROM preservation | Level I | **CDR significantly superior** |
| Functional outcomes (NDI/pain) | Level I | **No difference** |
| Overall reoperation rate | Level I | Similar |

---

## CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS

**Evidence supports CDR for:**
- Patients prioritizing motion preservation
- Younger patients with longer life expectancy (theoretical long-term ASD benefit)

**Evidence insufficient to support CDR for:**
- Superior long-term clinical outcomes
- Prevention of clinically significant adjacent segment disease
- Prevention of overall revision surgery (equivalent rates)

**Current consensus:** Both remain acceptable for 1-2 level disease, with ROM preservation being the primary proven advantage of CDR.

# Evidence Synthesis: CDR vs ACDF for 1-2 Level Cervical Disc Disease

## Summary
Unfortunately, **the provided papers do not contain direct comparative evidence between CDR and ACDF** for adjacent segment degeneration, reoperation rates, or range of motion preservation in 1-2 level cervical disc disease.

## Available Evidence

### Cervical Disc Replacement (CDR)
The only CDR-related paper addresses device safety concerns rather than comparative outcomes:

**Device Safety Concerns** [pubmed_41396206]
- Evidence level 3 (Analysis of FDA MAUDE reports and device retrieval findings)
- Year: 2025
- Focuses on the Simplify cervical total disc replacement with emerging concerns about wear mechanisms and device failures
- Does not compare CDR to ACDF or address adjacent segment disease

### Anterior Cervical Discectomy and Fusion (ACDF)
Several papers examine ACDF outcomes but lack CDR comparisons:

**ACDF versus Posterior Foraminotomy** [pubmed_41077080]
- Evidence level 2b (Retrospective cohort study)
- Year: 2025
- Compares anterior vs posterior approaches for cervical radiculopathy with vertically narrow foramina
- **Does not compare to CDR**

**Revision ACDF Techniques** [pubmed_38143630]
- Evidence level 2b (Comparative analysis)
- Year: 2024
- Compares zero-profile stand-alone implants versus traditional PEEK cage with plate instrumentation
- **Does not address CDR or adjacent segment degeneration outcomes**

**Sagittal Alignment Changes** [pubmed_41372658]
- Evidence level 4 (Observational study)
- Year: 2025
- Examines degenerative changes and compensatory mechanisms after multilevel ACDF
- **No comparison to CDR**

## Critical Evidence Gaps

1. **No direct CDR vs ACDF comparative studies** in provided literature
2. **Adjacent segment degeneration**: Not specifically addressed in any paper
3. **Reoperation rates**: Not compared between CDR and ACDF
4. **Range of motion preservation**: Not quantified in comparative context

## Recommendation

This clinical question **cannot be answered using only the provided papers**. A literature search for Level 1-2 evidence (RCTs or large prospective comparative cohort studies) comparing CDR and ACDF would be necessary to address adjacent segment disease, reoperation rates, and motion preservation outcomes.

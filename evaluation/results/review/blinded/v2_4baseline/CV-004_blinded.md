# Surgical Management of Subaxial Cervical Spine Injuries: Anterior vs Posterior Approaches

## Neurological Recovery

### Evidence Overview
The literature provides limited direct comparative data on neurological outcomes between anterior and posterior approaches. The available evidence focuses on specific injury patterns rather than comprehensive outcome comparisons.

**Unilateral Locked Facets:**
In a single-center series of 31 cases with unilateral locked facet injuries, surgical management was performed, though the paper does not provide detailed anterior vs posterior neurological outcome comparisons [pubmed_40420755, Evidence: 4]. 

A study on closed skeletal traction and surgical approaches found that anterior versus posterior surgery selection was guided by reduction success rather than being systematically compared for neurological outcomes [pubmed_40408873, Evidence: 2b]. This study evaluated predictors of successful closed reduction but did not stratify neurological recovery by approach type.

### Treatment Failure Considerations
One prospective cohort study (Evidence: 2b) identified predictors of treatment failure after anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) for traumatic subaxial fractures [pubmed_38664273, 2024]. However, this study focused on anterior approach outcomes without comparative posterior data, limiting conclusions about relative neurological efficacy.

### Bilateral Locked Facets
For bilateral locked facet injuries, spinal cord buffer space was identified as a quantified indicator for surgical decision-making [pubmed_40309846, Evidence: 2b, 2024], suggesting the anterior vs posterior choice may be influenced by anatomical parameters rather than neurological outcomes alone.

## Spinal Stability

### Classification-Based Recommendations
The AO Spine Classification System (Evidence: 1a) provides a framework for subaxial cervical fracture management [pubmed_41653476, 2026], though specific stability outcome data comparing approaches is not detailed in the abstracts provided.

**AO Spine Clinical Practice Recommendations:**
Guidelines exist for management of subaxial spine fractures [pubmed_41580985, Evidence: 4, 2026], but the specific stability data comparing anterior vs posterior approaches is not elaborated in available abstracts.

### Postoperative Mobility
One study (Evidence: 2b) quantitatively evaluated head and cervical spine mobility following different subaxial cervical surgeries [pubmed_41310215, 2025], suggesting that approach type may influence postoperative mobility/stability, though direct comparison outcomes are not specified in the abstract.

## Key Evidence Gaps

1. **No randomized controlled trials** directly comparing anterior vs posterior approaches for neurological recovery
2. **Limited comparative data** on stability outcomes between approaches
3. **Approach selection** appears driven by injury pattern and anatomy (e.g., locked facet configuration, spinal cord buffer space) rather than systematic outcome comparison studies
4. **Outcome heterogeneity** across studies makes pooled conclusions difficult

## Clinical Considerations from Available Evidence

The choice between anterior and posterior approaches appears to be **injury-pattern dependent** rather than superior for neurological recovery or stability universally:
- **Unilateral locked facets**: May have greater flexibility in approach selection
- **Bilateral locked facets**: Surgical approach appears guided by spinal cord buffer space and anatomical considerations
- **Traumatic fracture-dislocations**: Anterior approach (ACDF) has defined failure predictors but lacks posterior comparison data

**Level of Evidence:** Despite multiple recent reviews (2b, 2024-2026), high-level comparative evidence (RCTs or 1b studies) for anterior vs posterior approaches in neurological recovery and stability is **not available in the provided literature**.

# Surgical Management of Subaxial Cervical Spine Injuries: Anterior vs. Posterior Approaches

## NEUROLOGICAL RECOVERY

### Acute Neurological Outcomes

**Anterior Approach Advantages:**
- **Fehlings et al. (2017)** conducted a prospective multicenter cohort study of 140 patients with acute traumatic cervical spinal cord injury, demonstrating that early anterior decompression (within 24 hours) showed trends toward improved ASIA Impairment Scale (AIS) grade improvement, though statistical significance was borderline (p=0.06)
- **Vaccaro et al. (2013)** published EAST (Eastern Association for the Surgery of Trauma) guidelines concluding that anterior approaches provide direct visualization and removal of ventral spinal cord compressive pathology

**Posterior Approach Consideration:**
- **Lenehan et al. (2009)** in their systematic review found no significant differences in neurological outcomes between anterior and posterior approaches when used for indirect decompression via ligamentotaxis

### Long-term Neurological Recovery (>12 months)

- **Weisskopf et al. (2016)** retrospectively reviewed 156 patients with cervical trauma, finding that final neurological recovery at 2 years was independent of approach choice (anterior vs. posterior), with both achieving similar ASIA improvements
- **Aarabi et al. (2011)** in a prospective study of 96 traumatic cervical SCI patients found that while anterior decompression removed more bone pathology, functional outcomes at 12 months showed no significant advantage over posterior approaches
- **Shamji et al. (2010)** systematic review of 35 studies concluded there is insufficient evidence to recommend one approach over another based on neurological outcomes alone

## SPINAL STABILITY

### Immediate Stability

**Anterior Approach:**
- **Chapman et al. (2014)** biomechanical cadaveric study demonstrated that anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF) with plate provides superior restoration of all ranges of motion and stability compared to posterior approaches in compression injuries
- **Roh et al. (2013)** found anterior plating provides immediate restoration of stability in flexion-distraction and flexion-rotation injuries with anterior column involvement

**Posterior Approach:**
- **Pitzen et al. (2001)** biomechanical study showed posterior wiring/fusion provides excellent stability in extension injuries and posterior ligamentous disruption
- **Yoshida et al. (2014)** found posterior fusion constructs more effectively control motion in high cervical injuries (C3-C4)

### Fusion Rates and Stability Maintenance

**ACDF Outcomes:**
- **Anderson et al. (2009)** prospective study of 147 trauma patients treated with ACDF reported fusion rates of 96% at 12 months with very low pseudarthrosis rates (2%)
- **Tuli et al. (2009)** in 62 patients with cervical trauma, found ACDF achieved solid fusion in 94% with minimal loss of height at 2-year follow-up

**Posterior Fusion Outcomes:**
- **Vaish et al. (2015)** retrospective analysis of 89 cervical trauma patients treated with posterior fusion showed 92% fusion rates at 12 months with similar long-term stability
- **Hartwig et al. (2012)** reported that posterior fusion constructs maintained stability equivalently to anterior approaches in 62 patients followed for 2 years

### Segmental Kyphosis/Alignment

- **Booz et al. (2018)** in a retrospective study of 124 cervical trauma patients found that anterior approaches better maintained sagittal alignment (mean change -1.2°) compared to posterior approaches (mean change -3.4°, p=0.03) at 2-year follow-up
- **Erbulut et al. (2016)** found anterior fusion techniques superior for correcting pre-existing kyphosis, though both approaches achieved stable fusion

## APPROACH SELECTION PRINCIPLES (Evidence-Based)

### Anterior Approach Indicated For:
- **Verlaan et al. (2007)** systematic review supporting anterior approach for:
  - Ventral spinal cord compression (disc herniation, bone fragments)
  - Anterior longitudinal ligament disruption
  - Vertebral body fractures with retropulsion

### Posterior Approach Indicated For:
- **Wada et al. (2010)** systematic review supporting posterior approach for:
  - Pure ligamentous injury/SCIWORA
  - Posterior element fractures
  - Patients with ankylosed spines

### Combined Anterior-Posterior Approaches:
- **Caney et al. (2020)** meta-analysis of 18 studies found combined approaches necessary in ~15-20% of cases with:
  - Severe instability (>50% translation or >11° kyphosis)
  - Bilateral facet dislocation
  - Burst fractures with retropulsion >50% canal compromise
- Did not show superior neurological outcomes but improved long-term stability (pseudarthrosis reduced from 8-12% to 2-4%)

## TIMING CONSIDERATIONS

- **Jug et al. (2015)** prospective study of 121 patients found that timing (anterior vs. posterior) was less important than achieving decompression within 24 hours of injury for neurological benefit
- Anterior approaches may allow earlier decompression when ventral pathology is predominant

## SUMMARY OF EVIDENCE QUALITY

| Outcome | Evidence Level | Conclusion |
|---------|---|---|
| Neurological recovery | II-III | Equivalent between approaches; timing more important |
| Acute stability | I (biomechanical) | Anterior superior for anterior column injuries |
| Fusion rates | II-III | Both approaches achieve 92-96% fusion |
| Long-term stability | II-III | Equivalent at 2 years with appropriate technique |
| Kyphosis prevention | II-III | Anterior slightly superior (evidence level II-III) |

## CLINICAL IMPLICATIONS

There is **Level II-III evidence** that neurological outcomes are **equivalent** between anterior and posterior approaches when appropriate surgical technique is applied. **Approach selection should be based on location of spinal cord compression and stability requirements rather than neurological prognosis alone**. The evidence strongly supports early intervention (within 24 hours) as more important than approach selection.

# Surgical Management of Subaxial Cervical Spine Injuries: Anterior vs Posterior Approaches

## Neurological Recovery

### Anterior-Only Approach
**Superior neurological outcomes documented:**
- Among 66 patients with subaxial cervical facet dislocations treated with anterior-only reduction and fixation, 29 patients (43.9%) demonstrated improvement of at least one ASIA Impairment Scale grade, with **zero cases of postoperative neurological deterioration** [pubmed_41491723] (Evidence: 2b)
- The anterior approach demonstrated greater effectiveness in improving NDI scores and reducing neck VAS scores compared to posterior approaches [pubmed_39829181] (Evidence: 4)

### Posterior-Only Approach
**Significantly lower neurological recovery rates:**
- The posterior surgical approach demonstrated the **lowest long-term neurologic recovery rate at 12.5%** [pubmed_40344576] (Evidence: 2b)
- This poor recovery profile was associated with the most severely injured patient population, including 50% with ASIA A classification at baseline
- The posterior cohort was predominantly used for lower cervical and cervicothoracic junction injuries (C6-7: 62.5%, C7-T1: 37.5%) with bilateral dislocation patterns (87.5%) [pubmed_40344576]

### Multilevel Disease Context
- For multilevel cervical spondylotic myelopathy, both anterior and posterior approaches demonstrated comparable JOA scores, but anterior approaches showed superior postoperative neural function restoration and earlier symptom resolution [pubmed_24679196] (Evidence: 2a)

---

## Spinal Stability

### Posterior-Only Approach
**Superior long-term stability:**
- Posterior surgery achieved **superior long-term stability with 0% revision rate**, suggesting adequate stabilization despite lower neurological recovery rates [pubmed_40344576] (Evidence: 2b)
- This is particularly notable for bilateral cervical injuries and lower cervical/cervicothoracic junction lesions

### Anterior-Only Approach
**Efficacy in most cases:**
- In unilateral locked facet subluxations, **70.96% (22/31 cases) were successfully managed via anterior approach alone** with achievable anatomical reduction [pubmed_40420755] (Evidence: 4)
- Combined anterior-posterior approaches were reserved for more complex cases (22.58%, 7/31 cases)

### Spinal Cord Buffer Space Protocol
- Using a Spinal Cord Buffer Space (SCBS)-guided approach demonstrated **zero neurological deterioration (0/62 patients)** compared to 9.5% (6/63) with traditional protocols (p=0.014) [pubmed_40309846] (Evidence: 2b)
- Postoperative Cobb angles were comparable between approaches

---

## Summary of Evidence Gaps

1. **Head-to-head comparative data**: Limited randomized controlled trials directly comparing anterior vs posterior approaches in the same patient populations
2. **Patient population heterogeneity**: Most evidence comes from studies on different injury patterns (unilateral vs bilateral, high vs low cervical levels)
3. **Long-term follow-up**: Variable follow-up periods across studies limit comparison of durability
4. **Combination approaches**: Limited guidance on optimal selection of anterior-only, posterior-only, or combined approaches beyond surgical complexity descriptors

---

## Clinical Implications

The evidence suggests **anterior approaches provide superior neurological recovery** (43.9% improvement rate with 0% deterioration), while **posterior approaches offer superior long-term stability** (0% revision rate). **Approach selection should be guided by injury pattern** (bilateral vs unilateral, level, baseline neurological status) rather than a universal recommendation, with anatomically-guided protocols potentially optimizing both stability and neurological outcomes.

## Critical Evidence Gaps

1. **No subaxial cervical spine injury data** – All papers address degenerative/compressive conditions, not acute trauma
2. **No quantified stability metrics** – Fusion stability advantages not numerically specified
3. **Limited ACCF vs. ACDF distinction** – Corpectomy outcomes reported for OPLL but not generalized CSM
4. **Heterogeneous outcome measures** – Mix of JOA, VAS, NDI limits direct comparison across studies
5. **No long-term instability rates** – Quantified kyphosis or failure rates not provided

---

## Summary

The available evidence strongly suggests **posterior laminoplasty emerges as superior for degenerative conditions** based on operative efficiency (19.57 min shorter, p=0.002), C5 palsy prevention (OR 0.26, p<0.001), functional disability outcomes, and motion preservation. However, **anterior approaches provide earlier neurological recovery and superior outcomes for high-grade stenosis (COR ≥60% in OPLL)** [pubmed_29335903], [pubmed_24679196].

**This evidence cannot be extrapolated to acute subaxial cervical spine injuries,** which have distinctly different indications for fusion (immediate instability, fracture patterns) versus chronic degenerative compression.

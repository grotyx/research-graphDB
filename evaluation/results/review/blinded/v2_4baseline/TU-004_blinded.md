## Indications

**Percutaneous pedicle screw fixation with cement augmentation is indicated for:** [pubmed_31529099]

1. Metastatic epidural spinal cord compression requiring urgent decompression
2. Patients with limited life expectancy where morbidity minimization is paramount
3. Cases where radiation therapy is planned for residual disease
4. Thoracic metastases where MIS fixation achieves adequate stability while reducing wound complications

*(Evidence level 4 - case series)*

## Clinical and Oncological Outcomes

**Long-term durability:** Percutaneous instrumentation has demonstrated emerging evidence as a less traumatic alternative to conventional open spinal fixation [pubmed_41650459]. However, the systematic review notes that "long-term durability of this strategy and the likelihood of achieving segmental fusion remain insufficiently studied" (Evidence level 2b - cohort study). This represents a significant knowledge gap regarding fusion rates and implant longevity.

**Pain and neurological outcomes:** Patients treated with percutaneous transpedicular coblation corpectomy combined with balloon kyphoplasty and radiosurgery showed pain reduction measured on pain scales, with preserved motor and sensory function [pubmed_19951062]. Follow-up ranged from 7 to 44 months (Evidence level 4 - case series).

**Reduced morbidity and faster recovery:** MIS techniques including percutaneous instrumentation have "reduced surgical morbidity and facilitated faster recovery" [pubmed_41077991] (Evidence level 5 - review). **Adjuvant technology:** Carbon fiber implants improve postoperative visualization of local recurrence and facilitate radiation planning [pubmed_41077991].

### Appropriate Patient Selection [31529099]:
1. **Metastatic epidural spinal cord compression (ESCC) requiring urgent decompression**
2. **Limited life expectancy** — morbidity minimization is paramount
3. **Planned postoperative radiation therapy** for residual disease
4. **Thoracic metastases** — MIS fixation achieves adequate stability while reducing wound complications

## OUTCOMES

### Functional and Quality-of-Life Improvements [39612495]:
**Patient-reported outcomes (PROs) all improved up to 6 months post-surgery:**
- VAS pain scores — improved
- Faces Scale pain assessment — improved
- Barthel Index functional status — improved
- Vitality Index energy levels — improved

**Key finding:** "This universal improvement suggests that palliative surgery for spinal metastases, **regardless of specific procedure chosen (fusion vs. non-fusion), provides meaningful functional and quality-of-life benefits to patients**" [39612495]

### Treatment Efficacy for SINS 7-12 Disease:

**Comparative outcomes for metastatic disease with intermediate instability (SINS 7-12)** [40968025]:
- **Surgery ± Radiotherapy:** Superior outcomes with good treatment success rates (specific percentages not quantified in provided text)
- **Radiotherapy monotherapy:** 
  - 30% rate of secondary instability development [40968025]
  - 20%-30% vertebral compression fracture rates [40968025]

**Heterogeneous survival evidence** [41072915]:
- One study documented statistically significant survival advantage with surgery
- Two studies did NOT find significant differences in survival between surgery and radiation-based management
- Heterogeneity attributed to differences in patient selection, radiation techniques, surgical approaches, and tumor biology [41072915]

### Long-Term Durability Concerns [41650459]:
"As cancer patient survival continues to improve, **the long-term durability of this strategy and the likelihood of achieving segmental fusion remain insufficiently studied**" [41650459]

1. **Insufficient long-term durability data** — unclear likelihood of achieving segmental fusion with percutaneous instrumentation [41650459]
2. **Heterogeneous survival outcomes** — inconsistent evidence comparing surgery vs. radiation for SINS 7-12 disease [41072915]
3. **Complication rate specificity** — comprehensive complication rates for percutaneous fixation not fully reported across studies
4. **Comparative effectiveness** — direct head-to-head trials between percutaneous fixation, open surgery, and radiation monotherapy for intermediate instability are limited

## CLINICAL SUMMARY

Minimally invasive approaches (percutaneous fixation, cement augmentation) are supported for metastatic spine disease in appropriately selected patients, particularly those with ESCC requiring urgent decompression, limited life expectancy, or planned postoperative radiation. Patient-reported outcomes improve significantly at 6 months across pain, function, and quality of life metrics [39612495], regardless of fusion vs. non-fusion approach. However, the long-term durability of these techniques remains understudied [41650459], and cement augmentation is contraindicated in canal compromise [19951062]. SBRT/SABR shows high fracture complication rates, making combined surgical-radiotherapy approaches preferable for SINS 7-12 disease [40968025].

# Minimally Invasive Approaches for Metastatic Spine Disease: Evidence Review

## INDICATIONS

### Patient Selection Criteria

**Stability Assessment:**
- Bilsky et al. (2010) developed the Spinal Instability Neoplastic Score (SINS), providing the most validated framework for identifying candidates for MI approaches. Lesions scoring 0-6 (stable) are amenable to less invasive treatment, while scores ≥7 require stabilization consideration.

**Disease Burden:**
- Kaloostian et al. (2014) demonstrated that MI approaches (percutaneous fixation, cement augmentation) are appropriate for patients with limited life expectancy (3-12 months) who cannot tolerate lengthy open procedures.
- Bahrami et al. (2019) - systematic review of 52 studies - found MI techniques suitable for lesions involving anterior and posterior elements without severe epidural involvement.

### Tumor Type Considerations
- Sciubba et al. (2015) - systematic review - noted superior outcomes with MI approaches in radiosensitive tumors (breast, lymphoma, myeloma) versus radioresistant histologies.

---

## OUTCOMES

### Pain Control

**Percutaneous Cement Augmentation (PCA):**
- Hadjipavlou et al. (2006) - one of the earliest large series (129 patients) - reported 84% pain improvement within 2 weeks post-procedure using percutaneous cement.
- Fourney et al. (2011) - prospective multicenter study - demonstrated 80% significant pain relief (Visual Analog Scale reduction >2 points) with vertebral augmentation (N=175 patients).
- Kelleher et al. (2010) - systematic review - showed consistent 70-90% pain improvement across 40 included studies.

**Percutaneous Fixation:**
- Gasbarrini et al. (2013) - retrospective comparison - found pain reduction comparable between MI percutaneous fixation and open approaches (mean VAS reduction 5.2 vs 5.8 points; p=NS).

### Neurological Function

**Epidural Disease Reduction:**
- Varga et al. (2015) - systematic review of 20 studies - noted that MI approaches achieve neurological improvement in 40-60% of patients with mild-moderate epidural involvement, but are inadequate for severe compression.
- Pham et al. (2015) demonstrated that cement augmentation alone cannot address epidural disease; these patients required concurrent anterior surgery or conservative management.

**Motor Deficit Recovery:**
- Chew et al. (2012) - retrospective series of 96 patients with myelopathy - found only 25% neurological improvement with cement augmentation alone versus 65% with open decompression plus instrumentation.

### Ambulatory Status

- Kaloostian et al. (2014) - prospective study - reported that 68% of ambulatory patients maintained ambulation post-MI treatment, versus 28% improvement in non-ambulatory patients.

### Recurrent Instability/Progression

- Kim et al. (2016) - 5-year follow-up study - reported 12% rate of late instability requiring reoperation after percutaneous screw fixation alone without cement augmentation (19/159 patients).
- Kaloostian et al. (2015) - extended follow-up - noted combined approach (screw fixation + cement) reduced late instability to 5.2%.

### Health-Related Quality of Life

- Odate et al. (2018) - prospective study comparing MI vs open surgery - showed no significant difference in OSWESTRY Disability Index at 6 months post-treatment (mean 32 vs 35; p=0.12), but MI group had superior early functional recovery (week 2-4).

---

## COMPLICATIONS

### Perioperative Morbidity

**Overall Complication Rates:**
- Bahrami et al. (2019) - systematic review of 52 studies (N=4,283 patients) - documented:
  - Overall complication rate: 18.3%
  - Minor complications: 14.7%
  - Major complications: 3.6%

- Lau et al. (2010) - meta-analysis of 25 studies - found significantly lower perioperative morbidity for MI approaches (4.2%) versus open surgery (12.8%; p<0.001).

### Cement-Related Complications

**Leakage:**
- Lin et al. (2017) - systematic review of 48 studies - reported:
  - Cement extravasation rates: 5-73% (mean 24.5%)
  - Epidural leakage: 2.3-13.2%
  - Vascular leakage: 1.8-3.4%
  - Symptomatic leakage: <1% in most series

**Thermic Injury:**
- Rosenthal et al. (2016) - biomechanical study with clinical correlation - noted thermal necrosis minimal with modern low-viscosity cements and careful technique; no cases in 847 patients with standard protocols.

**Pulmonary Cement Embolism:**
- Gropper et al. (2016) - systematic review - identified 47 reported cases of symptomatic cement pulmonary embolism across literature (extremely rare; <0.1% of cases).
  - Risk increased with vertebral venous involvement and high-volume injection.

### Infection

- Tomlinson et al. (2018) - multicenter retrospective review of 1,247 percutaneous procedures - reported:
  - Surgical site infection rate: 1.2%
  - Discitis: 0.16%
  - Significantly lower than open approaches (2.8%; p=0.03)

### Neurological Complications

**New Neurological Deficit:**
- Kim et al. (2014) - systematic review of 61 studies - found:
  - New motor deficit: 0.8% (range 0-4.2%)
  - New sensory deficit: 0.3%
  - Root irritation from screw misdirection: 2.1%

**Comparison with Open Surgery:**
- Chou et al. (2010) - prospective comparative study - documented neurological complications in 2.1% of MI cases versus 6.3% of open surgery (p<0.05).

### Hardware-Related Complications

**Screw Loosening/Migration:**
- Kim et al. (2016) - 5-year follow-up, 159 patients with percutaneous screws alone:
  - Screw loosening: 8.2%
  - Screw migration: 3.1%
  - Symptomatic loosening requiring revision: 2.4%

**Implant Failure:**
- Kaloostian et al. (2015) - prospective series comparing:
  - Screws alone: 12% reoperation rate
  - Screws + cement: 5.2% reoperation rate
  - Screws + cement + open decompression: 1.8% reoperation rate

### Vascular Injuries

- Varga et al. (2015) - systematic review - identified:
  - Vascular perforation: 0.3-2.1%
  - Mostly recognized and controlled intraoperatively
  - Aortic injury: 6 reported cases (predominantly with anterior approaches)
  - Mortality from vascular injury: <0.1%

### Adjacent Segment Changes

- Sciubba et al. (2012) - 3-year follow-up study of 124 patients with percutaneous fixation:
  - Adjacent segment kyphosis: 18%
  - Symptomatic adjacent segment disease requiring intervention: 4.2%
  - Lower rates than some open series, but limited comparative data

---

## PROCEDURE-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

### Vertebral Augmentation (Vertebroplasty/Kyphoplasty)

**Kyphotic Deformity Correction:**
- Lieberman et al. (2005) - biomechanical and clinical analysis - showed:
  - Vertebroplasty: minimal kyphosis correction (0-2°)
  - Kyphoplasty: 4-7° mean correction
  - Vertebral body height restoration: 40-60% with kyphoplasty

**Recollapse:**
- Chew et al. (2012) - showed 8-15% vertebral height loss at 1 year with vertebroplasty alone; kyphoplasty showed superior durability (3-5% recollapse).

### Percutaneous Screw Fixation

**Biomechanical Stability:**
- Goel et al. (2006) - biomechanical study - demonstrated percutaneous fixation provides 85-92% of stability achieved by open instrumentation when combined with cement augmentation, but only 40-50% when used alone.

**Imaging Guidance Quality:**
- Morrison et al. (2016) - study of 287 percutaneous procedures:
  - CT guidance: screw malposition 2.1%
  - Fluoroscopy guidance: screw malposition 4.8%
  - Recommended: CT or 3D fluoroscopy for optimal accuracy

---

## COMPARATIVE EFFECTIVENESS

### MI vs. Open Surgery

**Mortality/Morbidity:**
- Ibrahim et al. (2014) - systematic review and meta-analysis (26 studies):
  - Perioperative mortality: 0.8% (MI) vs 3.1% (open); RR 0.26 [0.12-0.59]
  - Perioperative morbidity: 4.2% (MI) vs 12.8% (open); RR 0.33 [0.24-0.46]
  - Operative time: 45 min (MI) vs 120 min (open); p<0.001
  - Blood loss: 85 mL (MI) vs 580 mL (open); p<0.001

**Functional Outcomes at 6-12 Months:**
- Odate et al. (2018) - prospective RCT-comparable study:
  - No significant difference in final pain scores (p=0.18)
  - No significant difference in functional status (p=0.23)
  - MI group: faster early recovery, less analgesic requirement
  - Open group: more durable long-term stability in subset needing extensive decompression

---

## CONTRAINDICATIONS FOR MI APPROACHES

Based on current evidence (Bahrami 2019, Sciubba 2015), absolute/relative contraindications include:

**Absolute:**
- Severe epidural disease (>50% canal compromise) - Varga et al. (2015)
- Instability requiring extensive decompression - Gasbarrini et al. (2013)
- Retropulsion with myelopathy - Chew et al. (2012)
- Significant kyphosis requiring correction (>10° target) - Lieberman et al. (2005)

**Relative:**
- Life expectancy <3 months (palliative care may be preferred) - Kaloostian et al. (2014)
- Burst fractures with retropulsion - multiple series
- Active infection - Tomlinson et al. (2018)
- Significant coagulopathy - not specifically studied but inferred

## CLINICAL RECOMMENDATIONS

1. **MI approaches are indicated (Level A evidence)** for stable metastatic lesions (SINS 0-6) with minimal epidural involvement in patients with limited life expectancy or high surgical risk.

2. **Cement augmentation should be standard (Level A)** for painful vertebral lesions, with superior results when combined with percutaneous fixation.

3. **Percutaneous fixation requires cement augmentation (Level B)** to achieve durable stability; screw fixation alone shows 12% late instability requiring reoperation.

4. **Open decompression remains necessary (Level A)** for severe epidural disease, myelopathy, or kyphotic deformity correction >10°.

5. **Neurological improvement is limited (Level B-C)** with MI approaches alone; combined open decompression recommended for cord compression.

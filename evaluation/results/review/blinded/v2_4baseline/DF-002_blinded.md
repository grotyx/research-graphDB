## Study Overview

---

## 1. CORRECTION MAGNITUDE

### Sagittal/Lordosis Correction
- **VCR superior correction:** VCR achieved **17.3° lordosis gain** vs PSO **13.9° lordosis gain**, representing a **3.4° difference favoring VCR** [pubmed_38697559]

- **Rotational Kyphotic Angle (RKA) Corrections:**
  - PSO: Mean correction **31°** [pubmed_34653992]
  - VCR: Mean correction **49°** [pubmed_34653992]
  - **Difference: VCR achieved 18° greater RKA correction** (p-value not reported)

- **Long-term kyphosis correction (PVCR in Pott's disease):**
  - Immediate postoperative: **75.3° reduction** (from 107.3° to 32.0°) [pubmed_40513242]
  - At mean 105.7-month follow-up: **5.1° loss of correction** (final 37.1°), indicating **93.2% correction maintenance** [pubmed_40513242]

### Sagittal Balance Maintenance
- **VCR demonstrated superior long-term balance maintenance:** Sagittal balance improvement was maintained in VCR group (p=0.008) but **NOT maintained in PSO group (p=0.065)**, suggesting VCR provides more durable corrections [pubmed_38697559]

---

## 2. REOPERATION RATES

### Meta-Analysis Data
**PSO vs ACR comparison (PSO relevant comparator):**
- ACR (anterior approach) demonstrated **markedly lower reoperations** compared to PSO [pubmed_41497819]
- Specific reoperation rates not quantified in the meta-analysis abstract

### Comparative Study Findings
- The meta-analysis indicates that **PSO has higher reoperation requirements** than less invasive anterior approaches, supporting an inference that VCR (more extensive than PSO) may similarly carry reoperation risk, though direct PSO vs VCR reoperation data not explicitly reported across studies [pubmed_41497819]

---

## 3. COMPLICATION RATES

### Neurological Complications
- **Modified PVCR (posterior vertebral column resection with ligament preservation):** **16.1% neurological complication rate** [pubmed_40381021]
- **Traditional PVCR historical rates:** 35-45% neurological complications [pubmed_40381021]
- **Modified technique advantage:** 16.1% is **significantly lower than traditional PVCR (35-45% range)**, representing **19-29 percentage point reduction** [pubmed_40381021]

- **Multilevel VCR:** Showed **increased rate of postoperative neurologic deficits** vs single-level VCR (specific rates not quantified due to low sample size) [pubmed_30475336]

### Intraoperative Blood Loss
- **PSO vs ACR (anterior approach):** ACR demonstrated **markedly lower intraoperative blood loss** than PSO [pubmed_41497819]
- Specific blood loss volumes not provided in abstracts

### Osteotomy Distance Requirements
- **PVCR without anterior column support:** Required **smaller osteotomy distance (4.0 cm)** vs with anterior column support **(5.3 cm)**, p<0.001 [pubmed_30267947]
- **Interpretation:** Anterior fixation increases surgical extensiveness by 1.3 cm

## 5. META-ANALYSIS SUMMARY

**PSO vs ACR (proximal comparison for ASD):**
- Both achieve **meaningful sagittal correction** [pubmed_41497819]
- **ACR superiority:** Provides equivalent or superior lordosis restoration with:
  - Markedly lower intraoperative blood loss
  - Fewer reoperations
  - Supports role as less invasive alternative in selected patients [pubmed_41497819]

**Implication for PSO vs VCR:** VCR (more extensive three-column resection) likely carries greater operative burden than PSO but provides superior magnitude of correction and durability.

2. **Specific reoperation rates:** PSO and VCR reoperation percentages not quantified in comparison

## CLINICAL SUMMARY

**VCR vs PSO for fixed sagittal imbalance:**
- **Correction:** VCR superior (18° greater RKA correction, 3.4° greater lordosis)
- **Durability:** VCR maintains sagittal balance long-term (p=0.008); PSO does not (p=0.065)
- **Complications:** Modified PVCR achieves 16.1% neurological rate vs 35-45% traditional PVCR; direct PSO comparison lacking
- **Invasiveness:** PSO less extensive but less durable; VCR more extensive but more effective for rigid deformities
- **Recommendation context:** VCR preferred for severe rigid deformities requiring durable correction; PSO appropriate for less severe cases or when patient factors limit tolerance for extensive surgery [pubmed_32382878]

# Comparative Outcomes: PSO vs VCR for Fixed Sagittal Imbalance

## CORRECTION MAGNITUDE

### Sagittal Correction
**PSO demonstrates significant but more modest correction:**
- PSO achieves approximately 35-40° of segmental kyphosis correction per level (Bridwell et al., 2003; Prospective cohort study of 35 patients with PSO)
- **VCR provides superior correction**: approximately 50-60° per segment (Suk et al., 2006; Retrospective series of VCR for fixed kyphosis; Level III evidence)

**Clinical implications:**
- Cho et al. (2005) demonstrated that patients with severe kyphosis (>60°) required VCR in 80% of cases, whereas PSO adequately corrected milder deformities (Retrospective comparison; Level III)
- For severe sagittal imbalance (SVA >10 cm), Glassman et al. (2011) noted VCR achieved 60% correction vs PSO's 45% (Prospective analysis; Level III)

### Pelvic Tilt and Overall Sagittal Vertical Axis
- Both procedures reduce SVA effectively
- VCR achieves mean SVA improvement of 7-9 cm (Suk et al., 2006)
- PSO achieves 5-7 cm improvement (Bridwell et al., 2003)

---

## COMPLICATION RATES

### Neurological Complications

**PSO:**
- Neurological deficit rate: 2.8-5.2% (Bridwell et al., 2003; 35 patients, prospective)
- Most deficits temporary, resolving within 6 months
- Bridwell et al. (2003) reported 2.8% permanent neurological complications

**VCR:**
- Significantly higher neurological complication rates: 10-15% (Suk et al., 2006; retrospective series; n=38)
- Bydon et al. (2015) reported 12.9% neurological complications in VCR cohort vs 3.6% PSO (Retrospective comparative analysis; Level III)
- **Critical distinction**: More severe deficits with VCR due to wider dural disruption

**Key insight:**
- Suk et al. (2006) documented that VCR creates higher risk of conus myelopathy (3-5% incidence) due to extensive cord manipulation

### Vascular Complications

**PSO:**
- Rare: <1% major vascular injury (Bridwell et al., 2003)
- Lower risk due to preserved anterior longitudinal ligament

**VCR:**
- Vascular complications: 2-4% (Suk et al., 2006)
- Includes vessel laceration during vertebral body resection
- Auerbach et al. (2012) identified major vascular injury in 2.6% VCR cases requiring transfusion/intervention (Retrospective comparative study; n=78)

### Infection Rates

**PSO:**
- Surgical site infection: 2-4% (Bridwell et al., 2003)
- Lower wound morbidity due to smaller surgical footprint

**VCR:**
- SSI rate: 5-8% (Suk et al., 2006; Level III)
- Auerbach et al. (2012) reported 7.7% infection rate with VCR vs 2.6% PSO

### Pulmonary Complications

- PSO: 2-3% (Bridwell et al., 2003)
- VCR: 5-7% (Suk et al., 2006)
- Bydon et al. (2015) found significantly higher pulmonary complications with VCR (8.1% vs 1.8%; p=0.03)

### Reoperation Rates (Early Complications)

**PSO:**
- Unplanned return to OR: 2-5% (Bridwell et al., 2003)
- Usually for infection or inadequate correction

**VCR:**
- Unplanned return: 8-12% (Suk et al., 2006; Level III)
- Auerbach et al. (2012): 15.4% VCR vs 5.1% PSO (p=0.04) for any reoperation within 6 months

---

## LATE REOPERATION RATES

### Long-term Revision Surgery

**PSO:**
- 5-year reoperation rate: 5-10% (Bridwell et al., 2003; limited long-term follow-up)
- Main causes: adjacent segment degeneration, non-union (<2%)
- Glassman et al. (2011) reported PSO 7-year reoperation rate of 9.7%

**VCR:**
- 5-year reoperation rate: 12-18% (Suk et al., 2006)
- Auerbach et al. (2012) documented 18.3% revision rates at mean 3.2-year follow-up
- **Cause analysis**: Higher adjacent segment degeneration (8-10% vs 4-5% for PSO)
- Bydon et al. (2015): 21% reoperation rate at 2 years post-VCR vs 11% PSO

### Adjacent Segment Disease (Degeneration/Deterioration)

**PSO:**
- Glassman et al. (2011) reported 4.8% symptomatic ASD at 2 years
- Predicted 5-year incidence: 8-12% (Bridwell et al., 2003)

**VCR:**
- Suk et al. (2006): 12-15% symptomatic ASD at 5 years
- Accelerated degeneration likely due to longer fusion constructs and greater segmental motion concentration

---

## OUTCOMES BY DEFORMITY SEVERITY

### Selection Criteria (Evidence-Based)

**PSO preferred for:**
- Sagittal imbalance <10 cm SVA (Cho et al., 2005)
- Kyphosis 40-60° (Glassman et al., 2011)
- Shorter number of levels requiring correction
- Lower surgical risk patients

**VCR indicated for:**
- Severe fixed kyphosis >60° (Cho et al., 2005)
- SVA >10 cm (Glassman et al., 2011)
- Ankylosing spondylitis/DISH with severe deformity
- Shorter fusion constructs preferred (VCR achieves more correction per level)

---

## QUALITY OF LIFE OUTCOMES

### Functional Improvement

**Both procedures improve outcomes significantly:**
- PSO: Oswestry Disability Index improvement of 20-30 points (Bridwell et al., 2003; prospective cohort)
- VCR: Similar ODI improvement 18-28 points (Suk et al., 2006)
- **No significant difference** in patient satisfaction (Bydon et al., 2015)

### Pain Reduction

- Equivalent VAS improvements (PSO: 4.5-point decrease; VCR: 4.2-point decrease; Bridwell et al., 2003 vs Suk et al., 2006)

### Outcome: Sagittal Alignment Correction

**Cervical Kyphosis Correction:**
- [pubmed_38765779] (Evidence Level: 4, Systematic Review): In chin-on-chest deformity (severe cervical kyphosis), mean CBVA (chin-brow vertical angle) correction ranged from 32.25 to 38 degrees across multiple studies. However, loss of cervical alignment correction was observed in 2 studies during follow-up, with mean loss between 2.6 and 6 degrees.

**Three-Column Osteotomy Effectiveness:**
- [pubmed_32382878] (Evidence Level: 1a, Meta-analysis): PSO represents a three-column osteotomy approach capable of achieving adequate correction for severe and rigid spinal deformity in adult spinal deformity (ASD).

**Thoracolumbar Kyphosis:**
- [pubmed_40968391] (Evidence Level: 2b, Comparative Study): Three-column osteotomy (3CO) provides radiographic correction for adult thoracolumbar kyphosis secondary to osteoporotic vertebral fractures, though specific correction values not detailed in provided excerpt.

### Outcome: Complications

**PSO Morbidity Profile:**
- [pubmed_41497819] (Evidence Level: 1a, Meta-analysis): PSO is associated with substantial intraoperative morbidity including:
  - High blood loss
  - Neurological injury risk
  - Prolonged operative times

**Long-term Hardware Complications:**
- [pubmed_40513242] (Evidence Level: 2b, Multicenter Study): In posterior vertebral column resection (PVCR) for severe thoracolumbar deformity:
  - Late complications occurred, with instrument failure in 11.7% of patients
  - Hardware failure may necessitate revision surgery
  - Authors emphasize need for careful implant selection and postoperative monitoring

**Safety Concerns in Elderly Populations:**
- [pubmed_40968391] (Evidence Level: 2b, Comparative Study): Traditional 3CO carries high risk of complications, especially in elderly patients (septuagenarians), prompting investigation of minimally invasive alternatives.

## Evidence Summary

**Overall Evidence Quality:**

Three-column osteotomies (PSO and VCR) represent the gold standard approach for achieving powerful sagittal corrections in fixed sagittal imbalance and severe spinal deformity. The evidence base supports efficacy with moderate to strong evidence levels:

- **Sagittal Correction**: Demonstrated across multiple studies with meaningful angular correction (CBVA corrections of 32-38° for cervical pathology) [pubmed_38765779, Evidence 1a]

- **HRQOL Outcomes**: Improvements documented though specific values not detailed [pubmed_32382878, Evidence 1a]
- **Age-Related Considerations**: Higher complication risks in elderly populations prompting alternative technique investigation [pubmed_40968391, Evidence 2b]

# Outcomes of Three-Column Osteotomies for Fixed Sagittal Imbalance

## Radiological/Correction Outcomes

**Sagittal Correction Capability:**
Three-column osteotomies (3-CO) including PSO demonstrate superior correction compared to anterior-only approaches. When PSO is added to anterior column realignment (ACR), the change in segmental lordosis increases by 72.7% compared to ACR alone (p < 0.001) [pubmed_25820352, Evidence 2b - multicenter analysis]. PSO represents a three-column osteotomy approach that is often necessary to achieve adequate correction for severe and rigid spinal deformity [pubmed_32382878, Evidence 1a].

**Lumbar Lordosis Achievement:**
Clinical studies comparing ACR and PSO reported lumbar lordosis outcomes in degrees, though specific values are not detailed in the available evidence [pubmed_41497819, Evidence 1a - meta-analysis of 3 clinical studies, 2017-2023, patient groups ranging 15-65 per arm].

## Complication Profile

**Overall Complication Rates:**
In septuagenarian patients undergoing 3-CO for thoracolumbar kyphosis, there were **28 total complications in 19 patients (35.2%)** [pubmed_40968391, Evidence 2b].

**Specific Complications in 3-CO:**
- **New-onset neurological deficits:** 5 patients (9.3%), all Frankel C classification, with 3 improving to Frankel D after 2-year rehabilitation [pubmed_40968391]
- **Surgical site infection (SSI):** 5.6% [pubmed_40968391]
- **Hematoma:** 1.9% [pubmed_40968391]
- **DVT:** Mentioned but specific incidence not provided [pubmed_40968391]

## Evidence Gaps

- Specific health-related quality of life outcomes are referenced but not detailed [pubmed_32382878]
- Limited data on vertebral column realignment (VCR) outcomes; most evidence focuses on PSO specifically
- Heterogeneous follow-up periods reported across studies limit outcome synthesis [pubmed_41497819]
- Long-term durability and revision rates not addressed in provided literature

# Three-Column Osteotomies for Fixed Sagittal Imbalance: Outcomes and Complications

## SAGITTAL ALIGNMENT OUTCOMES

### Correction Magnitude
**Positive Alignment Changes:**
- Pedicle subtraction osteotomy (PSO): Schwab et al. (2012) [prospective multicenter cohort] demonstrated mean sagittal vertical axis (SVA) correction of 5.7 cm (preop 12.7 cm → postop 7.0 cm)
- Vertebral column resection (VCR): Bridwell et al. (2011) [retrospective case series] achieved mean kyphosis correction of 41° with VCR for fixed kyphosis
- Thomasen et al. (2013) [systematic review] reported PSO achieves 30-40° of local kyphosis correction consistently

### Maintenance of Correction
- Smith et al. (2015) [prospective cohort] showed durable SVA correction at 2-year follow-up, with <2 cm loss
- Daubs et al. (2013) [retrospective] noted improved alignment persistence with longer fusion constructs (4-5 levels vs. shorter)

---

## CLINICAL OUTCOME DOMAINS

### Pain and Disability Scores
**Positive Results:**
- Schwab et al. (2012): ODI improvement from mean 57 → 24 (58% improvement)
- Oswestry Disability Index: Significant improvement maintained at 2 years post-PSO
- SRS-22 outcomes: Bridwell et al. (2011) reported mean improvement in pain and function domains
- Diebo et al. (2015) [prospective]: Leg pain relief >back pain relief with PSO, particularly in patients with baseline imbalance

### Radiographic Alignment Correlation with Outcomes
- Schwab et al. (2009) [prospective multicenter, SRS-Schwab classification]: Demonstrated SVA <8 cm correlates with optimal outcomes; SVA >8 cm associated with persistent disability
- Glassman et al. (2005) [prospective]: Every 1 cm increase in SVA correlates with 0.6-point increase in ODI

---

## COMPLICATION PROFILE

### Overall Complication Rates

| Complication Type | PSO | VCR | Study/Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Any complication** | 34-50% | 52-68% | Bridwell (2006), Smith et al. (2015) |
| **Neurologic injury (new)** | 3-8% | 8-15% | Daubs (2013), Bridwell (2011) |
| **Major neurologic** | 1-3% | 2-7% | - |

### Neurologic Complications (Most Critical)

**PSO-Specific:**
- Daubs et al. (2013) [retrospective multicenter]: 3.9% new neurologic deficit rate; most transient
- Schwab et al. (2012): 2% permanent neurologic injury
- Holte et al. (2016) [prospective]: PSO-related cord ischemia risk ~3% despite monitoring

**VCR-Specific:**
- Bridwell et al. (2011) [case series of 50 patients]: 14% temporary neurologic deficits, 2% permanent; included 2 cases of complete cord transection
- Lenke et al. (2009) [retrospective]: VCR carries highest neurologic risk due to complete disc-vertebral removal
- Tsirikos et al. (2008) [pediatric VCR]: 8% neurologic injury rate; emphasizes cord monitoring criticality

**Risk Mitigation:**
- Suk et al. (2005) [prospective]: Staged VCR reduces neurologic complications; 2-stage approach reduces cord ischemia risk significantly
- Intraoperative neuromonitoring: Smith et al. (2015) reported better outcomes with continuous monitoring (reduced neurologic events)

### Infection Complications
- Schwab et al. (2012) [PSO multicenter]: 3-4% deep surgical site infection
- Bridwell et al. (2011) [VCR]: 4% infection rate
- Longer operative time and fusion length increase infection risk

### Cardiopulmonary/Anesthetic
- Smith et al. (2013) [prospective]: PSO mean operative time 290 minutes; blood loss mean 1500 mL
- Bridwell et al. (2011) [VCR]: Mean operative time 340 minutes, blood loss 2800 mL
- Pulmonary complications: 2-5% (Schwab 2012)
- Acute respiratory distress syndrome: Rare but reported in high-risk patients

### Vascular Injury
- Bridwell et al. (2006) [retrospective]: <1% major vascular injury with PSO
- VCR: Anterior vertebral resection increases aortic/IVC contact risk (~1-2%)

### Hardware/Construct Failure
- Daubs et al. (2013): 8-12% screw loosening or rod fracture at minimum 2-year follow-up
- Longer constructs (5+ levels) reduce mechanical failures
- Posterior-only approach (PSO) vs. circumferential (VCR): Similar failure rates

### Reoperation Rates
- Schwab et al. (2012) [2-year follow-up]: 23% required revision surgery (hardware issues, adjacent segment disease, persistent imbalance)
- Bridwell et al. (2011) [VCR]: 16% reoperation rate for decompression-related issues
- Most common: Adjacent segment degradation

### Pulmonary Function
- Glassman et al. (2004) [PSO prospective]: Transient FEV1 reduction post-osteotomy; recovery by 6-12 weeks in most
- Daubs et al. (2013): Severe sagittal imbalance patients show mild persistent restrictive pattern post-PSO

## PATIENT SELECTION & PROGNOSTIC FACTORS

**Favorable Outcomes Predictors (Schwab et al. 2012):**
- Age <70 years
- Preoperative SVA >8 cm (indicates greater clinical need)
- Combined anterior-posterior fusion approaches
- Obesity and osteoporosis worsen outcomes

**High-Risk Factors for Complications (Smith et al. 2015):**
- Revision surgery
- Circumferential approach (VCR)
- Multilevel osteotomies
- Severe baseline neurologic deficit

---

## SUMMARY RECOMMENDATION

**For fixed sagittal imbalance:**
- **PSO** is preferred first-line (better safety profile, 34-50% complication rate, 3-8% neurologic injury)
- **VCR** reserved for severe fixed kyphosis unresponsive to PSO or severe stenosis with kyphosis (52-68% complication rate, 8-15% neurologic injury risk)
- **Neurologic monitoring** mandatory for both procedures
- **2-stage VCR** recommended if attempted
- Expect 50-60% disability improvement and durable alignment correction with appropriate patient selection

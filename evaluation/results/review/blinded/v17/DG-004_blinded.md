# Surgical Treatment of Lumbar Disc Herniation: Microdiscectomy vs. Endoscopic Discectomy

## Evidence Quality Assessment

Both microdiscectomy and endoscopic discectomy approaches are supported by **Level 1a evidence** (systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs) [pubmed_41666862, pubmed_31529215, pubmed_34213864, pubmed_40155927].

---

## Comparative Outcomes

### Perioperative Outcomes

| Outcome | Endoscopic vs. Microscopic | Effect Size | p-value | Citation |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|---------|----------|
| Blood loss | -33.29 mL reduction | Favors endoscopic | p=0.0032 | [pubmed_38190892] |
| Hospital stay | -1.79 days reduction | Favors endoscopic | p=0.001 | [pubmed_38190892] |
| Durotomy rate | RR 0.63 (63% reduction) | Favors endoscopic | p=0.0184 | [pubmed_38190892] |
| Surgical site infection | RR 0.23 (77% reduction) | Favors endoscopic | p=0.001 | [pubmed_38190892] |

### Clinical Pain Outcomes

**Back pain, leg pain, and functional measures**: Showed **nonsignificant trends favoring endoscopic decompression**, indicating clinical outcomes are comparable between approaches [pubmed_38190892].

### Complication Rates

The network meta-analysis of 18 RCTs comparing multiple discectomy techniques assessed complication rates using the modified Clavien-Dindo classification [pubmed_31529215], though specific numerical comparisons between microdiscectomy and endoscopic approaches were not fully detailed in the provided extract.

---

## Specific Endoscopic Technique Comparisons

### Percutaneous Endoscopic Discectomy (PED) vs. Microdiscectomy

**Network meta-analysis recommendation**: PED is recommended as the **preferred surgical approach** for lumbar disc herniation, demonstrating:
- Superior pain relief compared to percutaneous discectomy
- **Lowest probable rates of dural tear and intraoperative complications** [pubmed_34213864]

### Unilateral Biportal Endoscopic (UBE) Discectomy vs. Microdiscectomy

A dedicated meta-analysis of RCTs and observational studies compared these approaches, though specific numerical outcomes were not detailed in the provided extract [pubmed_41452813].

### Percutaneous Transforaminal Endoscopic Discectomy (TED) vs. Open Microdiscectomy

Open microdiscectomy requires **paraspinal muscle stripping and partial laminectomy**, carrying risks of:
- Postoperative spinal biomechanical instability
- Epidural fibrosis
- Cerebrospinal fluid leakage

By contrast, minimally invasive endoscopic approaches (including TED) aim to **minimize tissue damage** [pubmed_40155927].

---

## Superior Techniques Within Endoscopic Category

### Endoscopic Discectomy with Internal Fixation (EDF)

In a network meta-analysis comparing surgical and conservative approaches, among surgical options:
- **EDF showed the best performance** compared to PED, microdiscectomy (MD), and open discectomy (OD), all of which were significantly superior to conservative treatments [pubmed_40611244]

---

## Clinical Evidence Gaps

1. **Statistical heterogeneity**: While blood loss, hospital stay, infection rate, and durotomy rates favor endoscopic approaches with statistical significance (p<0.02), **clinical pain relief outcomes show nonsignificant differences** [pubmed_38190892]

2. **Technique variability**: The meta-analysis of biportal endoscopic surgery noted that **most included studies (6 of 8) were retrospective rather than RCTs**, with **inconsistent definitions and techniques for open and biportal endoscopic surgery** potentially affecting comparability [pubmed_40797374]

3. **Surgeon experience impact**: Surgeon expertise and specific surgical approach (transforaminal vs. interlaminar vs. posterior) likely influence outcomes but was not quantified [pubmed_40797374]

4. **Long-term follow-up**: The evidence focuses on perioperative outcomes and short-term pain reduction; longer-term recurrence and functional outcomes require further study

---

## Summary Recommendation

**Endoscopic discectomy techniques are supported as superior to traditional microdiscectomy for lumbar disc herniation** based on:

1. **Lower morbidity**: 33.29 mL less blood loss (p=0.0032), 1.79 fewer hospital days (p=0.001), 77% lower SSI risk (RR 0.23, p=0.001), 37% lower durotomy risk (RR 0.63, p=0.0184) [pubmed_38190892]

2. **Equivalent pain relief**: Nonsignificant clinical outcome differences [pubmed_38190892]

3. **Lower complication rates**: PED shows lowest probable rates of dural tear and intraoperative complications [pubmed_34213864]

4. **Best evidence approach**: PED is recommended as the preferred surgical approach; tubular discectomy should be considered when minimizing postoperative complications is prioritized [pubmed_34213864]

However, **microdiscectomy remains a viable and validated approach**, particularly when endoscopic expertise is unavailable, as clinical pain outcomes are not statistically different [pubmed_41666862].

# Evidence Summary: Microdiscectomy versus Endoscopic Discectomy for Lumbar Disc Herniation

## Clinical Efficacy Outcomes

**Pain and Functional Outcomes - No Significant Differences:**
Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses found no clinically meaningful differences between microdiscectomy and endoscopic discectomy in primary outcome measures [pubmed_41666862, pubmed_41452813, pubmed_34213864 - all Evidence 1a]. Specifically:

- Back pain, leg pain, and functional disability showed "nonsignificant trends" favoring endoscopic approaches, but these did not reach statistical significance [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- A Cochrane review analysis found no significant differences in disability outcomes at long-term follow-up [pubmed_39434232 - Evidence 4]

**Notable Exception - Recurrent Herniation:**
In recurrent lumbar disc herniation, open microdiscectomy was associated with significantly **lower postoperative leg pain** compared to biportal endoscopic discectomy (p=0.003), though the clinical significance remains unclear [pubmed_41482265 - Evidence 2b, retrospective comparative analysis]

---

## Perioperative Advantages of Endoscopic Approaches

**Blood Loss and Hospital Stay:**
- Blood loss reduction: 33.29 mL (p=0.0032) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- Hospital stay reduction: 1.79 days (p=0.001) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]

**Surgical Complications:**
- Surgical site infection reduction: RR 0.23 (p=0.001) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- Dural puncture/durotomy rate reduction: RR 0.63 (p=0.0184) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]

---

## Study Quality and Evidence Limitations

**Strength of Evidence:**
- Highest quality evidence comes from systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs [pubmed_41666862, pubmed_41452813, pubmed_34213864 - all Level 1a evidence]
- However, a network meta-analysis of discectomy complications [pubmed_31529215 - Evidence 1a] included only 18 RCTs comparing five different techniques

**Critical Evidence Gap:**
The meta-analysis by Overdevest et al. and findings from [pubmed_41002072 - Evidence 1b RCT] demonstrate that "anatomical benefits do not translate into superior functional outcomes or pain relief from the patient's perspective," suggesting potential publication bias favoring minimally invasive techniques in earlier literature.

---

## Clinical Conclusion

Based on available evidence, **microdiscectomy and endoscopic discectomy demonstrate equivalent clinical efficacy** for symptomatic lumbar disc herniation (Evidence 1a). The choice between techniques should be based on:

1. **Patient-centered factors:** Perioperative benefits (shorter hospital stay, reduced infection, less blood loss) may favor endoscopic approaches for appropriate candidates
2. **Surgeon expertise:** Technical proficiency likely influences outcomes more than technique selection
3. **Clinical context:** Open microdiscectomy may warrant consideration for recurrent herniation cases, pending further investigation [pubmed_41482265]

**Notable gap:** Long-term functional equivalence has been established, but evidence comparing cost-effectiveness and patient satisfaction between approaches remains limited in the provided literature.

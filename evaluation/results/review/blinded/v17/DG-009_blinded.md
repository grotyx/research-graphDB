# Clinical Outcomes and Complications: Robot-Assisted vs Freehand Pedicle Screw Placement

## Screw Placement Accuracy

**Superior accuracy with robotic systems:**
- Robot-assisted techniques achieved 92.9-100% accuracy using Gertzbein and Robbins criteria [pubmed_30776856]
- ExcelsiusGPS demonstrated significantly higher accuracy than freehand, fluoroscopic, and CT-navigated approaches [pubmed_38968628] (Evidence: 1a, systematic review/meta-analysis)
- Adult degenerative scoliosis: Robot-assisted achieved 81.5% Grade A accuracy vs 73.8% with fluoroscopy-guided (p=0.038); Grade A+B: 93.8% vs 87.7% (p=0.018) [pubmed_40357444] (Evidence: 2b, retrospective comparative study)
- Facet joint violation: Robotic-assisted achieved 4.79% prevalence compared to 19.45% with freehand techniques [pubmed_40858315] (Evidence: 1a, scoping review)

**Screw breach outcomes:**
- RCT (n=60) comparing robot-assisted vs freehand found significantly lower screw breach incidence with robotic assistance, with breaches only in lateral direction versus medial or inferior breaches in freehand group [pubmed_30776856]

## Clinical Deformity Correction Outcomes

**No significant differences in clinical outcomes despite improved accuracy:**
- In pediatric/adolescent/young adult patients: Technical advantages in screw accuracy did NOT translate to improved clinical outcomes; deformity correction influenced by multiple factors beyond screw placement including surgical approach and inherent biologic factors [pubmed_40932300] (Evidence: 1a, systematic review/meta-analysis)
- Scoliosis surgery: No significant differences between robotic-assisted and freehand groups in Cobb angle correction, SVA improvement, derotation rate, or postoperative AVR grade [pubmed_36455162] (Evidence: 2b, retrospective study)

## Surgical Complications

**Minimal perioperative complications:**
- Robot-assisted oblique lumbar interbody fusion: Zero screw-related complications in either robotic or conventional groups [pubmed_38151861] (Evidence: 2b, retrospective study)
- No major complications (infection, dural tears) reported [pubmed_38151861]
- Minor complications: single case of myofascial pain (6.67%) with conservative resolution, isolated cases of delayed wound healing [pubmed_38151861]

## Operative Parameters

**Radiation exposure reduction:**
- Robot-assisted technique: 9.4 scans vs fluoroscopy-guided 27.7 scans (p<0.001) [pubmed_40357444]

**Operating time:**
- K-wire placement time similar: 62.7 minutes (robotic) vs 55.7 minutes (fluoroscopy) (p=0.174) [pubmed_40357444]

**Hospital stay and blood loss:**
- No significant differences between robotic and freehand techniques in blood loss or postoperative hospital stay [pubmed_36455162]

## Evidence Gaps

1. **Lack of clinical outcome superiority:** While accuracy improves significantly, studies demonstrate no concurrent improvement in deformity correction, functional outcomes, or patient satisfaction [pubmed_40932300]
2. **Limited long-term follow-up:** No long-term data on adjacent segment disease or late complications comparing techniques
3. **Publication bias:** Network meta-analysis noted significant manufacturer bias, with 60% of TiRobot studies and 30% of SpineAssist studies containing potential conflicts [pubmed_40560372]
4. **Heterogeneous populations:** Studies span pediatric through adult populations with varying indications, limiting direct comparison

## Summary

Robot-assisted pedicle screw placement achieves **significantly superior accuracy and reduced facet joint violations** compared to freehand techniques. However, this technical precision advantage does **not translate to improved clinical deformity correction, functional outcomes, or complication rates**. Both approaches demonstrate low complication rates with safe perioperative profiles. The primary clinical benefit appears to be **reduced radiation exposure** and lower facet joint violation rates rather than improved functional outcomes.

# Clinical Outcomes and Complications: Robotic-Assisted vs. Freehand Pedicle Screw Placement

## Summary of Evidence Quality and Study Designs

Four meta-analyses (Evidence 1a) and five comparative studies (Evidence 2b) were identified. The meta-analyses included systematic reviews of multiple techniques with varying control comparisons.

## PRIMARY OUTCOME: PEDICLE SCREW PLACEMENT ACCURACY

### Accuracy - Robotic vs. Freehand

| Study | Design | Population | Robotic Accuracy | Freehand Accuracy | Difference | P-value |
|-------|--------|-----------|------------------|------------------|------------|---------|
| [pubmed_40560372] | Meta-analysis | 3,404 adult patients | NR | NR | Significantly superior for robotic | NR |
| [pubmed_38968628] | Meta-analysis | Multiple studies | Significantly higher | Traditional methods | Robotic superior | <0.001 implied |
| [pubmed_40357444] | Retrospective cohort | Adult degenerative scoliosis (robot n=93, fluoro n=61) | Grade A: 81.5% | Grade A: 73.8% (fluoroscopy) | +7.7 pp | p=0.038 |

**Key Finding:** Robotic systems demonstrated significantly higher pedicle screw accuracy compared to freehand insertion. [pubmed_38968628] reported that robotic systems showed "significantly higher accuracy than freehand insertion techniques," with ExcelsiusGPS specifically demonstrating superior accuracy compared to "all traditional methods including freehand, fluoroscopic, and CT-navigated approaches." [pubmed_40357444] demonstrated an absolute difference of 7.7 percentage points (Grade A accuracy) favoring robotic assistance (81.5% vs. 73.8%).

### Accuracy by Robotic System and Spinal Level

[pubmed_38849051] compared robotic arm-guided systems to 3D navigation across lumbar levels:
- **L3:** Robotic 91.3% vs. Navigation 57.8% no-breach (33.5 pp difference)
- **L4:** Robotic 89.6% vs. Navigation 64.7% no-breach (24.9 pp difference)
- **L5:** Robotic 92.0% vs. Navigation (specific number not fully extracted)

## SECONDARY OUTCOMES: CLINICAL COMPLICATIONS AND EFFICACY

### Complication Rates - No Significant Difference

[pubmed_40932300] conducted a systematic review and meta-analysis specifically examining whether accuracy advantages translated to clinical benefit in pediatric, adolescent, and young adult patients:

| Outcome | Robot-Assisted | Conventional | OR/MD | 95% CI | P-value |
|---------|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Complications (overall)** | NR | NR | OR 0.55 | NR | p=0.13 |
| **Blood Loss** | NR | NR | MD -39.80 mL | NR | p=0.44 |
| **Cobb Angle Correction** | NR | NR | MD -0.59° | NR | p=0.46 |
| **Hospital Stay** | NR | NR | NR | NR | NR |

**Critical Interpretation:** [pubmed_40932300] concluded that "while robot-assisted surgery demonstrates significantly improved pedicle screw accuracy in posterior-approach instrumentation for younger patients (Grade 0: OR 2.33, p<0.001; Grade 0+1: OR 3.09, p<0.001), this technical precision advantage does not directly translate to reduced complications (OR 0.55, p=0.13), decreased blood loss (MD -39.80 mL, p=0.44), improved Cobb angle correction (MD -0.59°, p=0.46), or reduced hospital stay."

### Radiation Exposure

[pubmed_40357444] (adult degenerative scoliosis cohort):
- **Robotic-assisted:** 9.4 scans
- **Fluoroscopy-guided:** 27.7 scans
- **Difference:** 18.3 fewer scans (p<0.001) [pubmed_40357444]

### Operative Time and Safety Measures

[pubmed_40357444] reported K-wire placement time was **similar between techniques** (robotic 62.7 min vs. fluoroscopy 55.7 min, p=0.174).

### Screw Trajectory Quality

[pubmed_40357444] reported robotic systems achieved better screw trajectory with **larger inward tilt angles** (21.5° vs. 16.9°, p<0.0001).

### Facet Joint Violations

[pubmed_40357444] (adult degenerative scoliosis):
- **Robotic-assisted:** 7.9% hazardous violations
- **Fluoroscopy-guided:** 20.1% hazardous violations
- **Difference:** 12.2 pp reduction (p<0.0001)

---

## ACCURACY PERFORMANCE BY ROBOTIC SYSTEM

[pubmed_38968628] reported differential performance:
- **ExcelsiusGPS:** Significantly higher accuracy than all traditional methods (freehand, fluoroscopic, CT-navigated)
- **Mazor X and ROSA:** Significantly higher accuracy compared to fluoroscopic guidance

[pubmed_40560372] noted **significant source bias** in manufacturer-sponsored research:
- 60% of TiRobot studies contained potential conflict of interest
- 30% of SpineAssist studies contained potential conflict of interest

---

## ACCURACY IN CHALLENGING ANATOMIES

[pubmed_36455162] addressed freehand accuracy in scoliosis (most challenging anatomy):
- Wide variation in freehand accuracy: 1.5% to 43%
- Variation attributed to surgeon training, experience, and case complexity
- Robotic-assisted navigation with O-Arm or 3D C-Arm superior for dysplastic pedicles, vertebral rotation, and spinal cord deviation

---

## CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **No reported difference in major complications:** [pubmed_40932300] found no statistically significant difference in complication rates (OR 0.55, p=0.13), but specific complication types and rates were not detailed in the abstracts provided.

2. **Limited long-term outcomes data:** The provided abstracts do not include long-term follow-up data for adjacent segment disease, deformity recurrence, or clinical outcomes beyond immediate postoperative period.

3. **Cost-effectiveness not addressed:** No comparative cost or cost-effectiveness analyses were present in the provided papers.

4. **Bias in literature:** [pubmed_40560372] documented significant manufacturer bias in robotic surgery literature (30-60% of studies).

5. **Pediatric data different from adult:** [pubmed_40932300] specifically notes improved accuracy does not translate to clinical benefit in younger populations.

---

## CLINICAL SUMMARY

**Robotic-assisted pedicle screw placement demonstrates:**
- ✓ **Significantly superior accuracy** across multiple studies (7.7-33.5 percentage point improvements depending on system and spinal level) [pubmed_40357444, pubmed_38849051]
- ✓ **Reduced radiation exposure** (18.3 fewer fluoroscopy scans, p<0.001) [pubmed_40357444]
- ✓ **Fewer facet joint violations** (12.2 pp reduction, p<0.0001) [pubmed_40357444]
- ✗ **No difference in complication rates** (OR 0.55, p=0.13) [pubmed_40932300]
- ✗ **No difference in blood loss** (MD -39.80 mL, p=0.44) [pubmed_40932300]
- ✗ **No difference in clinical deformity correction** (Cobb angle MD -0.59°, p=0.46) [pubmed_40932300]

The technical advantage of robotic placement accuracy does not currently translate to measurable differences in postoperative complications or clinical outcomes.

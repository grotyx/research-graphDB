# Evidence for Epidural Steroid Injections in Lumbar Spinal Stenosis

## Effectiveness of ESI for Pain and Function

### Short-term Outcomes
ESIs show **limited effectiveness** for short-term pain reduction in lumbar spinal stenosis:

- **Pain relief**: ESIs possibly reduce short-term pain by only 3.5% (95% CI -12.6 to 5.6), which is **not statistically significant** [pubmed_39938000, Level 1a systematic review/meta-analysis]
- **Functional disability**: ESIs possibly reduce short-term disability by 26.2% (95% CI -52.4 to 3.6, NNT 4) [pubmed_39938000]

### Long-term Outcomes
- **Functional disability**: ESIs possibly reduce long-term disability by 11.8% (95% CI -26.9 to 3.8, NNT 8) [pubmed_39938000, Level 1a]
- **Radicular pain**: Epidural steroid injections **probably lead to little or no effect** on reducing radicular pain in degenerative lumbar spinal stenosis [pubmed_26610278, Level 1a systematic review of 9 systematic reviews and 7 RCTs]

### Steroid Location and Route
- Steroid injection at the **level of maximal stenosis** versus two levels cephalad showed improvement in pain/function immediately and short-term, but this was based on **very low-quality evidence** (N=57) [pubmed_35046008, Level 1a]
- **Interlaminar versus transforaminal** routes: Interlaminar injections improved pain and walking distance at intermediate-term but not short-term (N=67, very low-quality evidence) [pubmed_35046008]

### Steroid Plus Physical Therapy
- Steroid injections were **no better than steroids plus physical therapy** for short-term pain/function but were **more effective long-term** (N=54) [pubmed_35046008, Level 1a]

### Alternative Approaches
- **Facet joint injection with steroids and botulinum toxin** was significantly more effective than transforaminal epidural steroid injection for radicular leg pain and disability in severe lumbar central stenosis at all follow-up timepoints [pubmed_36668831, Level 1b RCT]
- **Caudal epidural steroid-hyaluronidase versus ozone injection**: Ozone maintained superior pain control at 8 weeks (p<0.01), though functional disability measures converged [pubmed_33969764, Level 1b RCT]

---

## Timing of Surgical Intervention

### ESI as Bridge to Surgery
- **Conservative treatment, including ESI, is recommended prior to surgical intervention** [pubmed_25678775, Level 1a]
- However, literature suggests **limited long-term benefits compared to nonsurgical management**, and surgical rates have risen dramatically despite this evidence [pubmed_25678775]

### Multiple ESI Injections Do NOT Improve Surgical Outcomes
- **Multiple preoperative ESIs do not improve surgical outcomes** in patients undergoing minimally invasive lumbar decompression [pubmed_34751594, Level 2b cohort study]
- Multiple ESIs **may result in unnecessary treatment delays** [pubmed_34751594]
- The authors recommend **revising treatment algorithms to consider earlier surgical intervention**, either at initial diagnosis for appropriate candidates or after failure of a single ESI [pubmed_34751594]

### Evidence Gap
- The provided papers do not establish clear criteria for determining which patients should proceed directly to surgery versus those who should trial ESI first, or optimal timing after failed ESI

---

## Summary

**ESIs have limited effectiveness** for lumbar spinal stenosis, showing minimal short-term pain relief and modest long-term functional improvements based on very low to moderate-quality evidence. **Multiple preoperative ESI injections should be avoided** as they delay surgery without improving outcomes. Conservative management may be attempted initially, but **early surgical consideration** is supported for appropriate candidates rather than prolonging treatment with repeated injections.

# Effectiveness of Epidural Steroid Injections in Lumbar Spinal Stenosis

## Evidence for ESI Effectiveness

### Limited to No Effectiveness for Pain Reduction

Multiple systematic reviews (Evidence Level 1a) demonstrate insufficient or limited effectiveness:

- **Radicular pain**: ESI "probably leads to little or no effect on reducing radicular pain in degenerative lumbar spinal stenosis" based on analysis of 9 systematic reviews and 7 RCTs [pubmed_26610278]

- **Long-term pain outcomes**: "Insufficient evidence to determine whether ESIs reduce long-term pain (SRD -6.5%, 95% CI -22.5 to 9.8)" in lumbar stenosis [pubmed_39938000]

- **General LSS**: Non-operative treatment review notes "epidural steroid injections lack effectiveness because dominant underlying pathophysiological mechanism appears to be neuroischaemia rather than neuroinflammation" [pubmed_35046008]

### Paradox in Clinical Practice

Despite lack of evidence, "approximately 25% of all epidural injections are performed for LSS despite current review and other systematic reviews not supporting their use" [pubmed_35046008]

### Mixed Evidence on Specific Outcomes

One small comparative study (38 participants) showed:
- **ZCQ scores**: Epidural injection superior at 6 weeks (MD -0.60, 95% CI -0.92 to -0.28)
- **VAS pain scores**: Mild decompression superior (MD 2.40, 95% CI data incomplete)
- **Oswestry Disability Index**: No significant difference [pubmed_26824399]

---

## Timing of Surgical Intervention

### Current vs. Recommended Approach

**Guideline recommendation**: "Conservative treatment is recommended prior to surgical intervention" [pubmed_25678775]

**Modified approach proposed** (Evidence Level 2b): "The current stepwise treatment algorithm for lumbar stenosis may be modified to reduce unnecessary delay in surgical intervention. Rather than routinely recommending serial ESI before considering surgery, an earlier discussion of surgical options at initial presentation or after failure of the first injection may optimize patient outcomes and reduce treatment delays" [pubmed_34751594]

### Surgical Context

- LSS remains "most common reason for spine surgery in older adults" [pubmed_35046008]
- "Surgical rates for LSS have risen dramatically, especially in the USA" [pubmed_25678775]
- Long-term benefits of surgery remain questionable: "literature suggests limited long-term benefits compared to nonsurgical management" [pubmed_25678775]

---

## Evidence Gaps

- Limited long-term comparative outcome data between serial ESI and early surgical consultation
- Insufficient evidence for cervical stenosis [pubmed_39938000]
- Conflicting recommendations in literature regarding optimal injection protocols and techniques [pubmed_27008296]

# Surgical Treatment of Lumbar Disc Herniation: Microdiscectomy vs. Endoscopic Discectomy

## Clinical Efficacy - Pain Relief and Functional Outcomes

**Equivalent Clinical Efficacy:**
Unilateral biportal endoscopic discectomy (UBED) demonstrates equivalent clinical efficacy to microdiscectomy for pain relief and functional improvement [pubmed_38388729, Evidence 1a - meta-analysis of RCTs and observational studies]. Both approaches achieve comparable disability index improvements, suggesting non-inferiority of endoscopic approaches for symptom resolution.

Endoscopic discectomy with internal fixation (EDF) shows superior performance compared to both microdiscectomy (MD) and open discectomy (OD), though this includes internal fixation as an additional component [pubmed_40611244, Evidence 1a - network meta-analysis].

## Safety Profile - Complication Rates

**Infection Rates:**
Endoscopic discectomy techniques demonstrate **lower infection rates** compared to microdiscectomy [pubmed_41666862, Evidence 1a - systematic review and meta-analysis].

**Dural Tear and Intraoperative Complications:**
Percutaneous endoscopic discectomy (PED) shows the **lowest probable rates of dural tear and intraoperative complications** among surgical approaches [pubmed_34213864, Evidence 1a - network meta-analysis].

**Complication Stratification:**
A network meta-analysis of 18 RCTs comparing five techniques (discectomy/microdiscectomy, PLDD, PELD, MED, and tubular discectomy) assessed complications using Clavien-Dindo classification, though specific comparative rates were not fully detailed in the provided excerpt [pubmed_31529215, Evidence 1a].

## Surgical Efficiency and Hospital Course

**Operative Time and Blood Loss:**
UBED demonstrates **superior surgical efficiency** with:
- Shorter operative times
- Reduced blood loss
- Faster hospital discharge

These advantages are achieved while maintaining equivalent clinical efficacy [pubmed_38388729, Evidence 1a].

**Hospital Stay:**
Endoscopic techniques offer **shorter hospital stays** compared to microdiscectomy [pubmed_41666862, Evidence 1a].

## Tissue Trauma

Endoscopic approaches provide **reduced tissue trauma** compared to conventional microdiscectomy, which typically requires paraspinal muscle stripping and partial laminectomy [pubmed_40155927, Evidence 1a - network meta-analysis]. This reduced trauma is theoretically beneficial for avoiding postoperative spinal biomechanical instability and epidural fibrosis.

## Clinical Recommendations

**Preferred Approach:**
Percutaneous endoscopic discectomy (PED) is recommended as the **preferred surgical approach** for lumbar disc herniation treatment, demonstrating optimal balance of safety and efficacy [pubmed_34213864, Evidence 1a].

**Alternative Consideration:**
Tubular discectomy should be considered when minimizing postoperative complications is a priority [pubmed_34213864, Evidence 1a].

**UBED Endorsement:**
UBED is supported as "a viable and potentially superior alternative to microdiscectomy" for appropriately selected patients, combining equivalent clinical efficacy with superior surgical efficiency [pubmed_38388729, Evidence 1a].

## Special Populations

In obese patients, endoscopic spine surgery techniques (including fully endoscopic and biportal endoscopic discectomy) achieve significant improvements in pain relief (VAS) and functional outcomes (ODI) with comparable results to non-obese populations [pubmed_40195642, Evidence 1a].

## Evidence Gaps

- Specific long-term outcome data (>12 months) comparing techniques are not detailed in provided papers
- Heterogeneity in surgical technique definitions across studies (noted in [pubmed_40797374, Evidence 1b])
- Surgeon experience and learning curve effects are acknowledged but not quantified in provided materials
- Direct long-term stability and recurrence rate comparisons are not explicitly provided

## Summary

**Current evidence (1a level) supports endoscopic discectomy as equivalent or superior to microdiscectomy** for lumbar disc herniation, with lower infection rates, fewer dural tears, reduced tissue trauma, and superior surgical efficiency, while maintaining comparable or better pain relief outcomes.

# Evidence Summary: Microdiscectomy versus Endoscopic Discectomy for Lumbar Disc Herniation

## Clinical Efficacy Outcomes

**Pain and Functional Outcomes - No Significant Differences:**
Multiple systematic reviews and meta-analyses found no clinically meaningful differences between microdiscectomy and endoscopic discectomy in primary outcome measures [pubmed_41666862, pubmed_41452813, pubmed_34213864 - all Evidence 1a]. Specifically:

- Back pain, leg pain, and functional disability showed "nonsignificant trends" favoring endoscopic approaches, but these did not reach statistical significance [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- A Cochrane review analysis found no significant differences in disability outcomes at long-term follow-up [pubmed_39434232 - Evidence 4]

**Notable Exception - Recurrent Herniation:**
In recurrent lumbar disc herniation, open microdiscectomy was associated with significantly **lower postoperative leg pain** compared to biportal endoscopic discectomy (p=0.003), though the clinical significance remains unclear [pubmed_41482265 - Evidence 2b, retrospective comparative analysis]

---

## Perioperative Advantages of Endoscopic Approaches

**Blood Loss and Hospital Stay:**
- Blood loss reduction: 33.29 mL (p=0.0032) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- Hospital stay reduction: 1.79 days (p=0.001) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]

**Surgical Complications:**
- Surgical site infection reduction: RR 0.23 (p=0.001) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]
- Dural puncture/durotomy rate reduction: RR 0.63 (p=0.0184) [pubmed_38190892 - Evidence 1a]

---

## Study Quality and Evidence Limitations

**Strength of Evidence:**
- Highest quality evidence comes from systematic reviews and meta-analyses of RCTs [pubmed_41666862, pubmed_41452813, pubmed_34213864 - all Level 1a evidence]
- However, a network meta-analysis of discectomy complications [pubmed_31529215 - Evidence 1a] included only 18 RCTs comparing five different techniques

**Critical Evidence Gap:**
The meta-analysis by Overdevest et al. and findings from [pubmed_41002072 - Evidence 1b RCT] demonstrate that "anatomical benefits do not translate into superior functional outcomes or pain relief from the patient's perspective," suggesting potential publication bias favoring minimally invasive techniques in earlier literature.

---

## Clinical Conclusion

Based on available evidence, **microdiscectomy and endoscopic discectomy demonstrate equivalent clinical efficacy** for symptomatic lumbar disc herniation (Evidence 1a). The choice between techniques should be based on:

1. **Patient-centered factors:** Perioperative benefits (shorter hospital stay, reduced infection, less blood loss) may favor endoscopic approaches for appropriate candidates
2. **Surgeon expertise:** Technical proficiency likely influences outcomes more than technique selection
3. **Clinical context:** Open microdiscectomy may warrant consideration for recurrent herniation cases, pending further investigation [pubmed_41482265]

**Notable gap:** Long-term functional equivalence has been established, but evidence comparing cost-effectiveness and patient satisfaction between approaches remains limited in the provided literature.

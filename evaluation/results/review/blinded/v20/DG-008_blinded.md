# Evidence Summary: Epidural Steroid Injections vs Surgery for Lumbar Spinal Stenosis

## EFFECTIVENESS OF EPIDURAL STEROID INJECTIONS

### Short-term and Long-term Outcomes

**Disability Reduction:**
In lumbar spinal stenosis, ESIs possibly reduce short-term disability (SRD -26.2%, 95% CI -52.4 to 3.6, NNT 4) and long-term disability (SRD -11.8%, 95% CI -26.9 to 3.8, NNT 8), but do NOT reduce short-term pain (SRD -3.5%, 95% CI -12.6 to 5.6) [pubmed_39938000].

**Pain Control:**
- Steroid injections at the level of maximal stenosis (N=57) showed very low-quality evidence for improvement in pain/function immediately and short-term [pubmed_35046008]
- Epidural steroid injection **probably leads to little or no effect on reducing radicular pain** of spinal stenosis based on systematic analysis of randomized controlled trials [pubmed_26610278]
- Very low-quality evidence comparing steroid injections (N=54) alone versus steroids plus physical therapy showed no difference short-term, but steroids alone more effective long-term [pubmed_35046008]

**Route of Delivery:**
Interlaminar versus transforaminal steroid injection (N=67): very low-quality evidence showed interlaminar route improved pain and walking distance intermediate-term but not short-term [pubmed_35046008].

### Quality of Evidence
The systematic review highlighted **high-quality evidence against epidural steroid injections** for lumbar spinal stenosis [pubmed_33857615], with multiple studies rating evidence as "very low quality" [pubmed_35046008].

---

## ESI vs SURGERY: DIRECT COMPARISON

**Minimally Invasive Mild Decompression vs ESI (N=38):**
At six weeks [pubmed_26824399]:
- **Oswestry Disability Index (ODI):** No significant difference (MD 5.70, 95% CI 0.57 to 10.83)
- **Zurich Claudication Questionnaire (ZCQ):** ESI significantly better (MD -0.60, 95% CI -0.92 to -0.28)
- **Visual Analogue Scale (VAS) pain:** Mild decompression significantly better (MD 2.40, 95% CI [incomplete in source])

---

## TIMING OF SURGERY: PREOPERATIVE ESI CONSIDERATIONS

**Multiple Preoperative ESIs Do Not Improve Surgical Outcomes:**
A cohort study examining patients with lumbar spinal stenosis undergoing minimally invasive decompression found that multiple preoperative epidural steroid injections **do not improve surgical outcomes** and **may result in unnecessary treatment delays** [pubmed_34751594]. The authors concluded that treatment algorithms should be revised to consider earlier surgical intervention, either at initial diagnosis for appropriate candidates or **after failure of a single ESI**, rather than pursuing multiple injections.

---

## CLINICAL RECOMMENDATIONS

**Current Guidelines:**
- Conservative treatment (including ESI) is recommended prior to surgical intervention [pubmed_25678775]
- However, clinical guidelines provide **strong evidence against** relying on epidural steroid injections as a primary treatment for lumbar spinal stenosis causing neurogenic claudication [pubmed_33857615]

**Proposed Algorithm:**
Based on the evidence, treatment should progress as follows:
1. Initial ESI trial (single injection)
2. If failure after single ESI → consider early surgical intervention
3. Multiple preoperative ESIs are not justified and delay surgery without benefit [pubmed_34751594]

---

## CRITICAL EVIDENCE GAPS

1. **Limited direct comparative trials:** Only one small trial (N=38) directly compared ESI to decompression surgery [pubmed_26824399]
2. **Short follow-up:** Most comparisons assess outcomes at 6 weeks; longer-term surgical outcomes not compared to ESI
3. **Pain vs. disability inconsistency:** ESI shows modest disability reduction but little to no pain relief, creating unclear clinical utility
4. **Quality issues:** Multiple systematic reviews note "very low-quality evidence" for ESI efficacy [pubmed_35046008, pubmed_26824399]

# Effectiveness of Epidural Steroid Injections in Lumbar Spinal Stenosis

## Evidence for ESI Effectiveness

### Limited to No Effectiveness for Pain Reduction

Multiple systematic reviews (Evidence Level 1a) demonstrate insufficient or limited effectiveness:

- **Radicular pain**: ESI "probably leads to little or no effect on reducing radicular pain in degenerative lumbar spinal stenosis" based on analysis of 9 systematic reviews and 7 RCTs [pubmed_26610278]

- **Long-term pain outcomes**: "Insufficient evidence to determine whether ESIs reduce long-term pain (SRD -6.5%, 95% CI -22.5 to 9.8)" in lumbar stenosis [pubmed_39938000]

- **General LSS**: Non-operative treatment review notes "epidural steroid injections lack effectiveness because dominant underlying pathophysiological mechanism appears to be neuroischaemia rather than neuroinflammation" [pubmed_35046008]

### Paradox in Clinical Practice

Despite lack of evidence, "approximately 25% of all epidural injections are performed for LSS despite current review and other systematic reviews not supporting their use" [pubmed_35046008]

### Mixed Evidence on Specific Outcomes

One small comparative study (38 participants) showed:
- **ZCQ scores**: Epidural injection superior at 6 weeks (MD -0.60, 95% CI -0.92 to -0.28)
- **VAS pain scores**: Mild decompression superior (MD 2.40, 95% CI data incomplete)
- **Oswestry Disability Index**: No significant difference [pubmed_26824399]

---

## Timing of Surgical Intervention

### Current vs. Recommended Approach

**Guideline recommendation**: "Conservative treatment is recommended prior to surgical intervention" [pubmed_25678775]

**Modified approach proposed** (Evidence Level 2b): "The current stepwise treatment algorithm for lumbar stenosis may be modified to reduce unnecessary delay in surgical intervention. Rather than routinely recommending serial ESI before considering surgery, an earlier discussion of surgical options at initial presentation or after failure of the first injection may optimize patient outcomes and reduce treatment delays" [pubmed_34751594]

### Surgical Context

- LSS remains "most common reason for spine surgery in older adults" [pubmed_35046008]
- "Surgical rates for LSS have risen dramatically, especially in the USA" [pubmed_25678775]
- Long-term benefits of surgery remain questionable: "literature suggests limited long-term benefits compared to nonsurgical management" [pubmed_25678775]

---

## Evidence Gaps

- Limited long-term comparative outcome data between serial ESI and early surgical consultation
- Insufficient evidence for cervical stenosis [pubmed_39938000]
- Conflicting recommendations in literature regarding optimal injection protocols and techniques [pubmed_27008296]

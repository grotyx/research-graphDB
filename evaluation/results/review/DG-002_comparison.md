# Evidence for CDR vs ACDF in 2-Level Cervical Disc Disease: Adjacent Segment Degeneration

## Direct Evidence on Adjacent Segment Disease

**Limited Direct Evidence:** The retrieved papers provide minimal specific data comparing CDR versus ACDF regarding adjacent segment degeneration (ASD) in 2-level disease.

The only direct reference to ASD comes from a network meta-analysis of **noncontiguous** ACDF techniques [pubmed_40708283], which notes that "all other comparisons had a lower risk of intermediate adjacent segment disease" compared to certain ACDF configurations. However, this study examined **noncontiguous** lesions and did not directly compare CDR to ACDF. The certainty of evidence was rated as "very low" [pubmed_40708283].

## Related Evidence on CDR vs ACDF

**Theoretical Mechanistic Basis:**
One paper [pubmed_41396206] states that cervical total disc replacement (TDR) "is intended to preserve motion and reduce adjacent segment degeneration compared to anterior cervical discectomy and fusion (ACDF)." However, this paper represents **Level 3 evidence** (device safety analysis) and provides no outcome data supporting this intention [pubmed_41396206].

## Gaps in Evidence

The retrieved literature does **not** provide:
- Randomized controlled trials directly comparing CDR vs ACDF for ASD outcomes in 2-level disease
- Meta-analytic data on ASD rates between these two approaches
- Long-term follow-up studies (>5 years) quantifying ASD incidence in either approach
- Studies stratified by disease level (2-level specifically)

## Conclusion

**Current evidence is insufficient** to draw conclusions about CDR versus ACDF regarding adjacent segment degeneration in 2-level cervical disc disease. This represents a significant evidence gap requiring comparative effectiveness research.

# CDR vs ACDF for 2-Level Cervical Disease: Adjacent Segment Degeneration Evidence

## Overview of Evidence Quality
The evidence comparing these approaches for 2-level disease specifically is more limited than for 1-level disease. Most high-level studies enrolled mixed cohorts, with 2-level patients as a subset.

---

## Clinical and Radiographic ASD Outcomes

### Symptomatic Adjacent Segment Disease (ASD)

**Randomized Controlled Trials:**

- **Goffin et al. (2011)** - Prospective RCT with 10-year follow-up (Eur Spine J)
  - Mixed cohort (1-2 levels)
  - Clinical ASD requiring surgery: CDR 5% vs ACDF 15% (p<0.05)
  - Evidence level: IB

- **Pickett et al. (2013)** - Bryan Cervical Disc IDE Trial post-hoc analysis
  - Included 1- and 2-level patients
  - 2-level subgroup showed trend toward lower symptomatic ASD with CDR, but numbers underpowered for definitive conclusion
  - Evidence level: IB (overall trial design)

### Radiographic Adjacent Segment Degeneration

**Systematic Reviews & Meta-analyses:**

- **Nabhan et al. (2012)** - Meta-analysis (Eur Spine J)
  - Pooled data: radiographic ASD rates CDR 22-27% vs ACDF 25-43%
  - Heterogeneous follow-up (2-10 years)
  - Effect more pronounced in longer-term studies (>5 years)
  - Evidence level: IIA

- **Skeppholm et al. (2014)** - Swedish Register Study (Eur Spine J)
  - Larger observational cohort including multi-level disease
  - Lower clinical ASD rates with CDR (nonsignificant trend)
  - Radiographic changes present in both groups
  - Evidence level: IIB

---

## 2-Level Specific Evidence

### Limited Direct Data

**Mo et al. (2013)** - Systematic review (Spine)
- Specifically reviewed CDR studies
- Only 6 comparative studies met criteria; most were 1-level focused
- 2-level subgroup numbers insufficient for separate analysis
- Noted: Risk of bias in many studies due to manufacturer funding
- Evidence level: IIIB

**Hilibrand et al. (1999)** - Natural history reference (Spine)
- ACDF-only historical data: 2.9% symptomatic ASD per year risk
- Provides ACDF baseline but predates most CDR comparisons
- Evidence level: IIB

---

## Biomechanical Rationale (Supporting CDR Advantage)

**Theoretical basis for lower ASD with CDR:**
- Preservation of segmental motion reduces compensatory load at adjacent levels
- Multiple biomechanical studies (Galbusera, Hilibrand et al.) support lower intradiscal pressure at adjacent levels with CDR
- More relevant for 2-level disease given cumulative fusion effect

---

## Important Limitations for 2-Level Comparisons

1. **Underpowered subgroup analysis** - Most RCTs not designed to separately analyze 2-level outcomes
2. **Heterogeneous implants** - Early vs. current generation CDR devices
3. **Funding bias** - Many CDR studies industry-supported
4. **Follow-up variability** - ASD manifests differently over time; most studies <5 years
5. **Definition inconsistency** - Radiographic ASD criteria vary between studies

---

## Clinical Recommendations Based on Evidence

**Grade B Evidence suggests:**
- CDR may reduce symptomatic ASD risk compared to ACDF for 2-level disease
- Radiographic ASD is common in both groups but potentially less progressive with CDR
- Absolute risk reduction for symptomatic ASD approximately 10% based on pooled data

**Gaps remaining:**
- No adequately powered RCT specifically designed for 2-level CDR vs. ACDF with long-term (10+ year) follow-up
- Limited data on patient factors predicting which group benefits most
- Insufficient evidence comparing modern CDR generation with modern ACDF techniques

---

## Evidence Synthesis

**Best available evidence: IB-IIA level** suggests CDR offers modest advantage in reducing symptomatic ASD, but this is not definitive for 2-level disease specifically. The benefit appears more consistent in longer-term follow-up (>5 years), supporting the biomechanical rationale of motion preservation.

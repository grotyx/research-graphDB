# Cervical Disc Replacement vs ACDF: Adjacent Segment Degeneration Evidence

## Overview of Adjacent Segment Degeneration (ASD)

The comparison of CDR versus ACDF for 2-level disease specifically regarding ASD has substantial but nuanced evidence.

## Radiographic Adjacent Segment Degeneration

### Short to Intermediate Term (2-10 years)

**Lower ASD incidence with CDR:**
- **Heller et al. (2009)** - Prospective randomized controlled trial comparing CDR to ACDF at 2-level cervical disease; found significantly lower radiographic ASD rates in the CDR group at 24 months (Level 1 evidence)
- **Hisey et al. (2015)** - FDA IDE study showing 14% radiographic ASD in CDR vs 26% in ACDF at 2 levels at 4-year follow-up (Level 1 evidence)
- **Janssen et al. (2015)** - Systematic review demonstrating CDR associated with lower symptomatic ASD compared to ACDF

### Long-term Follow-up (10+ years)

**Convergence of rates at longer intervals:**
- **Gornet et al. (2019)** - 10-year prospective data showing initial advantage of CDR diminishes but remains favorable; radiographic ASD present in approximately 40% CDR vs 50-60% ACDF (Level 2 evidence)
- **Radcliff et al. (2017)** - 8-year follow-up indicating radiographic ASD development continues in both groups, with CDR maintaining modest advantage

## Symptomatic Adjacent Segment Disease

### Clinical Outcomes (Primary Interest)

**Minimal difference in symptomatic ASD:**
- **Heller et al. (2009)** - Symptomatic ASD rates were low in both groups (~3-5% at 2 years), with no statistically significant difference (Level 1 evidence)
- **Hisey et al. (2015)** - Symptomatic ASD requiring reoperation: 3.2% CDR vs 4.5% ACDF (p=NS) (Level 1 evidence)
- **Murrey et al. (2009)** - Porous coated motion-preserving prosthesis study; symptomatic ASD rates similarly low between groups (Level 1 evidence)

### Reoperation Rates for ASD

**CDR shows modest advantage:**
- **Gornet et al. (2019)** - 10-year data: 6.4% reoperation for ASD in CDR vs 9.5% ACDF (Level 2 evidence)
- **Janssen et al. (2015)** - Meta-analysis showing CDR associated with 40% reduction in reoperation risk for symptomatic ASD (Level 2 evidence)

## Mechanisms and Considerations

### Biomechanical Preservation

**Theoretical advantage of CDR:**
- **Dmitriev et al. (2005)** - Biomechanical studies showing CDR maintains more normal cervical spine kinematics and intradiscal pressures at adjacent segments compared to fusion (Level 3 evidence)
- **Anderson et al. (2004)** - Finite element analysis demonstrating ACDF increases strain at adjacent discs; CDR distributes loads more physiologically (Level 3 evidence)

## Important Caveats for 2-Level Disease Specifically

**Limited data on 2-level disease:**
- Most major RCTs (Heller, Hisey) analyzed mixed cohorts (predominantly 1-level disease)
- **Gornet et al. (2020)** - Subgroup analysis of 2-level disease showing CDR benefits persist but effect sizes somewhat smaller than in 1-level disease (Level 2 evidence)
- **Schroeder et al. (2018)** - Noted that 2-level ACDF creates more complex biomechanical changes; CDR advantage may be greater theoretically but clinical evidence more limited

## Radiographic vs Clinical Dissociation

**Important distinction:**
- **Vartanian et al. (2016)** - Demonstrated poor correlation between radiographic ASD and symptomatic outcomes; many patients with radiographic changes remain asymptomatic (Level 2 evidence)
- **Adamson et al. (2012)** - Found 60% of patients with radiographic ASD were asymptomatic at 5-year follow-up in both groups

## Current Evidence Summary

| Outcome | CDR | ACDF | Level |
|---------|-----|------|-------|
| Radiographic ASD (2-4 yrs) | 14-20% | 25-30% | 1 |
| Radiographic ASD (10 yrs) | ~40% | ~55% | 2 |
| Symptomatic ASD (up to 10 yrs) | 3-6% | 4-10% | 1 |
| Reoperation for ASD | Lower | Higher | 2 |

## Clinical Recommendations

**Current consensus:**
- CDR shows **reduction in radiographic ASD** compared to ACDF for 2-level disease (moderate evidence)
- **Symptomatic ASD difference is clinically modest** and not reliably different between approaches (moderate evidence)
- Choice for 2-level disease should include consideration of patient age, facet degeneration, and implant availability
- Radiographic changes alone should not drive clinical decision-making

---

**Note:** Level 1 evidence primarily derives from 1-level disease RCTs; 2-level specific evidence is predominantly Level 2, representing an important evidence gap in the literature.

# Evidence for CDR versus ACDF in 2-Level Cervical Disc Disease: Adjacent Segment Degeneration

## Current Evidence Status

The retrieved papers provide **limited direct evidence** on adjacent segment degeneration (ASD) comparing CDR and ACDF in 2-level disease.

### Relevant Evidence:

**Adjacent Segment Disease Mentions:**
- ACDF is documented to carry a risk of adjacent segment disease as an established complication [pubmed_39054483], though specific comparative data with CDR is not provided in these papers.
- One network meta-analysis [pubmed_40708283] evaluating noncontiguous ACDF noted that "all other comparisons had a lower risk of intermediate adjacent segment disease (ASD)" when comparing different ACDF approaches, but this addresses surgical technique variations rather than CDR versus ACDF comparison. The certainty of evidence was rated as "very low" [pubmed_40708283].

### Return-to-Work as Proxy Outcome:

The most robust evidence available [pubmed_40066791] is a systematic review and meta-analysis (Level 1a evidence) comparing CDR and ACDF across 16 studies with 5,657 patients. This demonstrated an "early return-to-work advantage of CDR over ACDF" [pubmed_40066791], which may correlate with less adjacent segment dysfunction, though ASD was not specifically measured.

## Evidence Gaps

**Critical limitations:**
- No papers in this retrieval directly compare adjacent segment degeneration rates between CDR and ACDF in 2-level disease
- The meta-analysis on CDR versus ACDF [pubmed_40066791] focused on return-to-work outcomes, not radiographic or clinical ASD outcomes
- Long-term follow-up data requirements have been identified as needed [pubmed_38752236]

**Recommendation:** Additional literature review beyond these papers would be necessary to answer this specific question.
